## 1. General Introduction (1.1 Reamhra)

- **The book's purpose** is to describe the sounds of the Irish language (phonetics and phonology) for beginners.
- **Phonetics (foghraiocht)** – only **articulatory phonetics** is considered: how consonants and vowels are produced.
- **Phonology (foineolaiocht)** – **phonemics (foineimniocht)** is considered: the phoneme as the minimal unit that distinguishes word meaning.

## 2. Dialects of Irish (1.2 Canuinti na Gaeilge)

- Irish has three main dialect regions:
  - **Ulster (Cuige Uladh)** – represented by **Gaeilge Ghaoth Dobhair (GD)** (County Donegal).
  - **Connacht (Cuige Chonnacht)** – represented by **Gaeilge na Ceathrun Rua (CR)** (County Galway).
  - **Munster (Cuige Mumhan)** – represented by **Gaeilge Chorca Dhuibhne (CD)** (County Kerry).

- For each dialect, one informant was selected, whose speech the descriptions and transcriptions in the book are based on.
- **Important for G2P:** differences between dialects are significant; there is no single oral standard.

## 3. Orthography and Sounds (1.3 Litriu agus fuaimeanna na Gaeilge)

- The book describes **real sounds of living speech**, not what can be derived from orthography.
- Orthography is not always reliable for pronunciation due to dialectal differences.
- **Example of orthographic ambiguity (important for G2P):**
  - The letter sequence **⟨ei⟩** should, by rule, yield a long vowel **/e:/** (as in *beil*).
  - However, in **unstressed syllables** it yields a **short /e/** (the example in the text is incomplete, but *beil* in unstressed position is implied).
  - **Conclusion:** the rules of "orthography → pronunciation" have exceptions that depend on stress and position.

## Key Terms (for understanding G2P tasks)

- **Carballú (palatalisation)** – secondary articulation: raising the middle part of the tongue toward the hard palate (denoted by [ʲ] or by the lenition sign in Irish studies).
- **Coguasú (velarisation)** – raising the back part of the tongue toward the soft palate (denoted by [ˠ]).
- **Sleamhnóg (glide)** – a transitional vowel that arises between a consonant and a vowel of different quality (e.g., [j] or [w]).
- **Fóinéim (phoneme)** – the minimal unit that distinguishes meaning (example in the text: *bán* /bV/ vs *dán* /dV/).
- **Allafón (allophone)** – a positional variant of a phoneme (e.g., [x], [h], [xʷ] for one phoneme in GD).

## 2. Introduction to Articulatory Phonetics (2.1 Réamhrá)

- **Articulatory phonetics (foghraíocht urlabhraíochta)** describes how speech sounds are produced.
- The main focus is on features important for conveying meaning.

---

## 2.2 Organs of Speech and Voicing (2.2 Orgáin an ghutha agus glóriú)

### 2.2.1 Lungs
- **Lungs (scamhóga)** – the source of airflow for most sounds (egressive flow).

### 2.2.2 Trachea, Larynx, Pharynx
- Air passes: lungs → **trachea (traicé)** → **larynx (laraing)** → **pharynx (faraing)** → mouth.

### 2.2.3 Vocal Folds and Voicing (Téada an ghutha, glóriú)
- **Vocal folds (téada an ghutha)** – two muscles in the larynx.
- **The gap between them – the glottis (glotas)**.
- When the folds are closed and air passes through them, they vibrate – this produces **voicing (glórú)**.

### 2.2.4 Voicing as a Meaning-Distinguishing Feature
- **Example of a minimal pair** (Irish):
  - *an-chaol* [an χi:l] (very narrow) – *an-ghaol* [an γi:l] (very relationship)
  - The difference is only in voicing: /χ/ (voiceless) vs /γ/ (voiced).

- **Important for G2P**:
  - **Consonants** can be **voiced (glórach)** or **voiceless (neamhghlórach)**.
  - **Vowels** are always voiced.

### 2.2.5 Vocal Tract (Conair an ghutha)
- Parts above the larynx: pharynx, oral cavity, nasal cavity.

### 2.2.6 Articulators (Baill urlabhartha)
- **Active (gníomhach)** – movable organs (e.g., lower lip, tongue).
- **Passive (éighníomhach)** – immovable (e.g., upper teeth, palate).

#### Table 2.1: Articulators and Place Labels (copied from the text)

| Passive | Active | Place Label |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| Upper lip | Lower lip | **Bilabial (déliopach)** |
| Upper teeth | Lower lip | **Labiodental (liopadhéadach)** |
| Upper teeth | Tongue tip | **Dental (déadach)** |
| Alveolar ridge | Tongue blade | **Alveolar (ailbheolach)** |
| Hard palate | Front of tongue | **Palatal (carballach)** |
| Soft palate | Back of tongue | **Velar (coguasach)** |
| Pharynx | Tongue root | **Pharyngeal (faraingeach)** |
| – | Vocal folds | **Glottal (glotach)** |

---

## 2.3 Places of Articulation (2.3 Áiteanna urlabhartha)

### 2.3.1 Definition
- Consonants are distinguished by the **place where the obstruction is created**.
- **Example of a minimal pair** (distinction by place):
  - *fáil* (ring) – *sáil* (heel) – difference: /f/ (labiodental) vs /s/ (alveolar).

### 2.3.2 List of Places of Articulation with Examples (from the text)

1. **Bilabial (déliopach)** – closure of the lips.
   - Examples: *poill, pá, buí, bí, móin, meoin*.
2. **Labiodental (liopadhéadach)** – lower lip to upper teeth.
   - Examples: *faoi, fí, bhuí, bhí*.
3. **Dental (déadach)** – tongue tip to the back of the upper teeth.
   - Examples: *cat, tá, gad, dó, nocht, locht*.
4. **Alveolar (ailbheolach)** – tongue tip or blade to the alveoli.
   - Examples: *tae, suí* (and English *Tay, day*).
5. **Postalveolar (iar-ailbheolach)** – between the alveoli and the hard palate.
   - Examples: *deas, leas, seas, teas, sí*.
6. **Palatal (carballach)** – front of the tongue to the hard palate.
   - Examples: *giall, ciall, ghiall, chiall, falaing*.
7. **Velar (coguasach)** – back of the tongue to the soft palate.
   - Examples: *gad, cad, an-ghaol, an-chaol, falang*.
8. **Glottal (glotach)** – at the glottis.
   - Examples: *hata, shona*.

---

## 2.4 Manners of Articulation and Nasal Consonants (2.4 Cineálacha urlabhartha agus consain shrónacha)

- **Manner of articulation (cineál urlabhartha)** – the type of contact or action at the place of articulation.

### 2.4.1 Plosive (pléascach) = stop
- Complete closure, pressure build-up, release.
- Examples in Irish: *bá, pá, dá, tá, gá, cá*.

### 2.4.2 Nasal (srónach, pléascach srónach)
- Complete closure in the mouth, but the soft palate is lowered → air goes through the nose.
- Examples: *Máire, imeall, náire, inneall, i ngála, i ngeall ar*.

### 2.4.3 Tap / Trill (cnagach / tríleach)
- **Tap (cnagach)** – a single strike of the tongue against the alveoli (Irish *r*).
- **Trill (tríleach)** – a series of strikes (in some dialects).
- Examples: *rí, Brian*.

### 2.4.4 Fricative (cuimilteach)
- Incomplete closure; air passes through a narrow opening with friction.
- Examples: *faoi, fé, bhuí (in Munster), bhí, suí, sí, an-chaol, chiall, an-ghaol, ghiall, hata*.

### 2.4.5 Approximant (neasach) = semivowel (leathghuta)
- Wide opening; the sound is close to a vowel.
- Examples: *bhán (outside Munster), weather, whether, ghiall (in Ulster), yet, run*.

### 2.4.6 Lateral Approximant (neasach taobhach) = lateral (taobhach)
- Closure in the center; air flows along the sides of the tongue.
- Examples: *luí, ligh*.

**Summary for describing a consonant:**
It is necessary to specify (1) **place**, (2) **manner**, (3) **voicing**, (4) **secondary articulation** (see below).

---

## 2.5 Vowels (2.5 Gutai)

### 2.5.1 Difference from Consonants
- In vowels, there is no narrowing of the articulators; quality is determined by the **shape of the tongue**.

### 2.5.2 Vowel Parameters
- **Position of the highest point of the tongue (barrphointe na teanga)**:
  - **By backness (tulsáite / aistarraingthe)**: front (tosaigh) / back (cúil).
  - **By height (ard / íseal)**: high (ard/dúnta) / low (íseal/oscailte).
- **Lip shape**: rounded (cruinn) / unrounded (scartha).
- **Length (fad)**: long (fada) / short (gearr).

### 2.5.3 Examples of Front Vowels (sequentially from high to low)
- *dí* – high front, *di* – ?, *Dé* – mid-high front, *deis* – mid-low front, *deas* – low front.

### 2.5.4 Examples of Back Vowels (from low to high)
- *carr* – low back, *cór* – mid-low back, *cor* – mid-high back, *cur* – high back, *cúr* – high back rounded.

### 2.5.5 Length as a Meaning-Distinguishing Feature
- **Example of a minimal pair**:
  - *gás* [ga:s] (gas) – *gas* [gas] (stem) – difference only in vowel length.

### 2.5.6 Diphthong (défhoghar)
- A vowel during which the tongue moves during production.
- Examples: *fia, fuath, fadhb* (outside Ulster), *fabht* (and English *bye, boy, bough*).

---

## 2.6 Secondary Articulation (2.6 Urlabhairt thánaisteach)

- In Irish, almost every consonant has **secondary articulation**.
- Two main types:

### 2.6.1 Palatalisation (carballú)
- The tongue takes the shape as for the vowel *í* (high front).
- The body of the tongue rises toward the hard palate.
- Examples: *ghiall, yesterday*.

### 2.6.2 Velarisation (coguasú)
- The tongue takes the shape as for the vowel *ú* (high back rounded).
- The body of the tongue rises toward the soft palate.
- Examples: *cur, cúr, ghaolta*.

**Summary for describing a consonant in Irish:**
It is necessary to specify: place + manner + voicing + palatalisation/velarisation.

## 3.1 Introduction (Réamhrá)

- **Phonetic transcription (tras-scríobh foghraíochta)** is a tool for recording speech sounds in a concise, convenient, and standardized way.
- Usually, only **phonologically significant** features are noted in transcription.
- **Phonology (fóineolaíocht)** studies the sound system of a language and the rules of their interaction.
- **Phonological analysis** allows one to identify the set of distinct sounds (phonemes), which influences the choice of transcription.

---

## 3.2 Phonetic Transcription and IPA (Tras-scríobh foghraíochta agus an IPA)

### 3.2.1 History
- **The International Phonetic Association (IPA)** was founded in 1886.
- The **International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)** was developed – standardized symbols for speech sounds.

### 3.2.2 Examples of IPA Symbols
- **[d]** – voiced alveolar plosive (as in English *door*).
- **[ð]** – voiced dental fricative (as in English *the*).

### 3.2.3 IPA Tables in the Book
- **Figure 3.1** – IPA table for consonants (Consain).
- **Figure 3.2** – IPA table for vowels (Gutaí) – the "vowel quadrilateral" (ceathairshleasán na nglanghutaí).
  - Vertical axis: tongue height.
  - Horizontal axis: backness (front – left, back – right).
  - For each pair: left – unrounded, right – rounded.
- **Figure 3.3** – IPA diacritics (comharthaí idirdhealaitheacha).

### 3.2.4 Correspondence of IPA and Orthography (important for G2P)
- Many IPA symbols coincide with Latin letters (intuitively understandable).
- **Example of mismatch** (Irish):
  - The orthographic sequence **⟨ch⟩** in the word *chuaigh* denotes a **voiceless velar fricative**.
  - The IPA symbol for this sound is **[x]** (not obvious for Irish readers unfamiliar with IPA).

### 3.2.5 Diacritics (examples for Irish)
- **[ʷ]** – labialization (rounding) as secondary articulation.
  - Example: in *máthair* (Gaeilge Ghaoth Dobhair) [ʷ] is added to some consonants.
- **[̪]** – dental plosive (e.g., Irish *d* in *dán* – [d̪]).
- **[ʲ]** – palatalisation (e.g., the transitional sound [j] between a consonant and a vowel).
- **[j]** – palatal approximant.
- **[w]** – labial-velar approximant.

## 3.3 Phonology and Phonemic Transcription (Fóineolaíocht agus tras-scríobh fóinéimeach)

### 3.3.1 Phoneme (fóinéim)
- A unit that distinguishes the meaning of words.
- **Minimal pair (péire íosmhéide)** – two words that differ by only one phoneme.

### 3.3.2 Example of Phonemic Contrast
- **bán** /bV/ (white) vs **dán** /dV/ (poem) – difference in the first phoneme.
- In transcription, phonemes are placed **between slashes** / /.

### 3.3.3 Allophones (allafóin)
- Different phonetic realizations of one phoneme that do not change meaning.
- **Example** (Irish, Gaoth Dobhair):
  - Beginning of word *mo chos* – [x] (voiceless velar fricative)
  - Middle of word *clocha* – [x] or [h]
  - Before /t/ in *seacht* – [xʷ] (labialized)
  - All these variants are allophones of one phoneme /x/.
- It is recommended to choose for a phoneme the symbol of the most frequent allophone (or the simplest to write).

### 3.3.4 The Problem of Standard Pronunciation in Irish
- In English there is **Received Pronunciation (RP)** – a standard.
- In Irish, there is **no single oral standard** – only regional variants.
- Therefore, analysis is done separately for the three main dialects:
  - **Gaoth Dobhair (GD)** – Ulster Irish
  - **An Cheathrú Rua (CR)** – Connacht Irish
  - **Corca Dhuibhne (CD)** – Munster Irish

## 3.4 Consonants of Irish (Consain na Gaeilge)

- Irish has a **large number of consonants** due to meaning-distinguishing secondary articulation (palatalisation / velarisation).
- Almost all consonant phonemes are organized into **pairs of velarized vs palatalized**.
- In some dialects there are three types of **l** (voiced) and three types of **n** (voiced), as well as voiceless sonorants (nasals, approximants, laterals, rhotics) – in the book they are treated as phonemes (e.g., /mʷ/), although their theoretical status may be debatable.

### 3.4.1 Examples of Minimal Pairs (from tables 4.1, 5.1, 6.1, mentioned in the text)
- /pʷ/ vs /pʲ/ – *scóp* vs *scóip* (in CR)
- /bʷ/ vs /bʲ/ – *buí* vs *bí*, *buíon* vs *bíonn*, *bó* vs *beo*
- /tʷ/ vs /tʲ/ – *tuí* vs *tí*
- /dʷ/ vs /dʲ/ – *dó* vs *deo*
- /k/ vs /c/ – *cúis* vs *ciumhais*
- /g/ vs /j/ – *gal* vs *geal*
- /fʷ/ vs /fʲ/ – *faoi* vs *figh*
- /sʷ/ vs /ʃ/ – *suí* vs *sí*
- /x/ vs /ç/ – *an chú* vs *an chiú*
- /mʷ/ vs /mʲ/ – *óm* vs *óim*
- /nʷ/ vs /nʲ/ – *naoi* vs *nigh*
- /rʷ/ vs /rʲ/ – *cur* vs *cuir*
- /lʷ/ vs /lʲ/ – *lón* vs *leon*, *luí* vs *leigheas*

This proves the phonemic status of palatalisation/velarisation.

## 3.5 Vowels of Irish (Gutaí na Gaeilge)

- Vowels are distinguished by **length** (phonemically significant).
- **Diphthongs (défhoghair)** – a sequence of two vowels with a smooth transition.

### 3.5.1 Examples of Diphthongs (in CR)
- *dian* – /iə/ (in the book /io/ in some notations)
- *cadhain* – /ai/
- *sleamhain* – /au/
- *tuar* – /uə/

### 3.5.2 Minimal Pairs for Diphthongs (CR)
- *dian* vs *díon*
- *cadhain* vs *cáin*
- *sleamhain* vs *sleáin*
- *tuar* vs *túr*

### 3.5.3 Glides (sleamhnóga) – extremely important for G2P
- They arise from the combination of a palatalized consonant with a back vowel or a velarized consonant with a front vowel.
- They are not phonemes (do not change meaning), but are phonetically distinct.
- They are written as **superscript symbols** (e.g., [ʲ], [ʷ] or [i], [u]).

#### Rules for the Appearance of Glides (from the text):

1. **Back vowel after a palatalized consonant**:
   - *pioc* [pʲiək] (CD) – glide [i] between pʲ and ə?

   In the text: *pioc* [pih1Akh] (CD) – there is a complex notation. Essentially, [j] or [i] insertion.

   Refined examples from section 3.5:
   - When a back vowel follows a palatalized consonant: a **glide [ʲ]** (or [i]) appears between them – e.g., *pioc* [pʲiək].

2. **Palatalized consonant after a back vowel**:
   - *coipthe* [kʌˈpʲiː] (CR) – after a back vowel before a palatalized consonant, [j] or [i] appears? In the text: *coipthe* [kh1A'pih1i:] (CR). Complex, but the gist is a glide [i] or [j].

3. **Front vowel after a velarized consonant**:
   - *puiteach* [pʷˈuːtʲəx] (CD) – glide [u] or [w] between pʷ and u. In the text: *puiteach* [pYh1u1ih1ax] (CD). That is, [u] as a glide.

4. **Velarized consonant after a front vowel**:
   - *scríobtha* [ʃcɾʲiːbʷə] (GD) – glide [w] or [u] between iː and bʷ? In the text: *scríobtha* [cch1pih1:9pYh1a] (GD) – complex.

**Simplified rule for G2P:**
- **Cʲ + back vowel** → insertion of [j] (or [i])
- **Back vowel + Cʲ** → insertion of [j] (or [i])
- **Cʷ + front vowel** → insertion of [w] (or [u])
- **Front vowel + Cʷ** → insertion of [w] (or [u])

These glides are not written in phonemic transcription, but are obligatory for phonetic accuracy.

## 4.3.1 Front Vowels (Gutai tosaigh)

- Before velarized consonants, vowels are slightly retracted.
- Between two velarized consonants, vowels are slightly centralized.

**/iː/** [iː] – unrounded front high long (close to cardinal [i]).
- Also occurs with orthographic ⟨ao⟩, ⟨aoi⟩: *naoi*, *naomh*, *saol*, *taobh*.

**/ɪ/** [ɪ] – unrounded near-front near-high short.

**/eː/** [eː] – unrounded front mid-high long.

## Key Takeaways for the G2P Script from Chapter 4 (GD)

1. **GD consonants** have phonemic pairs distinguished by velarisation / palatalisation. In G2P it is necessary to distinguish, e.g., /pʷ/ vs /pʲ/, /t̪ʷ/ vs /tʲ/, etc.
2. **Allophonic changes:**
   - Plosives /pʷ, t̪ʷ, k, c/ are aspirated [ʰ] at the beginning of a syllable, except after /s/.

## 7.1 Introduction (Réamhrá)

- Irish orthography is **opaque** (teimhneach) in terms of a direct "letter → sound" mapping, but **relatively regular** (réasúnta rialta). Rules can be formulated.
- **Grapheme (graiféim)** – a letter or sequence of letters. Written in angle brackets: ⟨a⟩.
- **Opacity** is partly due to **morphological transparency** (trédhearcacht moirfeolaíochta): orthography preserves morphemic structure, even if pronunciation changes.
  - **Example:** initial mutation (athrú tosaigh) – after the preposition *í* the word *cuisneoir* (refrigerator) changes /k/ to /g/, but is written *i gcuisneoir* (preserving the spelling of the initial consonant).
  - **Example:** verb ending – *tógtha* [t̪ʷoːkʲiː] (CR) – orthography preserves ⟨th⟩, although it is pronounced [iː].

## 7.2 Consonant Graphemes (Graiféimí consanta)

### Basic Principle of Denoting Palatalisation / Velarisation

- The Irish alphabet has 13 consonant letters: **b, c, d, f, g, h, l, m, n, p, r, s, t**.
- The distinction between **velarized** and **palatalized** consonants (phonemically significant) is conveyed by **neighboring vowel letters**:
  - **Broad (velarized)** consonant – next to **⟨a, á, o, ó, u, ú⟩** (back/open).
  - **Slender (palatalized)** consonant – next to **⟨e, é, i, í⟩** (front).

- **Example:** *Sadhbh* (female name) – begins with broad /sʷ/ (before ⟨a⟩). *Seán* (male name) – begins with slender /ʃ/ (before ⟨e⟩). Minimal pair: *suí* [sʷiː] vs *sí* [ʃiː].

- **Quality harmony in clusters (braisle):** consonants in a cluster must be of the same quality (all broad or all slender). There are exceptions.

### Exceptions to the Quality Rules

- **⟨s⟩** at the beginning of a word before **⟨f, m, p⟩** is always broad (velarized):
  - *sféar* [sʲfʲeːr]? No, the text says: *sféar, sméar, spéir* – initial /s/ is broad even before slender? Actually: "Bíonn (s) i dtús focail cogusaithe go fóinéimeach i gcónaí roimh (f), (m), nó (p)" – that is /sʷ/ before ⟨f, m, p⟩, even if the following vowel is slender? This is an exception: orthographic ⟨s⟩ in such clusters does not change quality to slender.

- **⟨r⟩** at the beginning of a word or before ⟨d, l, n, t, s⟩ is always broad (velarized).

### Denotation of Voiceless Sonorants (consain shondacha neamhghlóracha)

- A voiceless sonorant (e.g., /m̥/, /n̥/, /l̥/, /r̥/) is conveyed by adding **⟨th⟩** or **⟨f⟩** after the sonorant letter, or **⟨sh⟩** before it.
  - **Example:** *cromtha* [kɾʌm̥ə] (CD) – ⟨mth⟩ → /m̥/.
- In the book, such voiceless sonorants are treated as separate phonemes (e.g., /mʷ/ for voiceless /m̥ʷ/), although their status may be debatable.

### General Principle Summarizing the Tables:
- **A simple consonant letter** (b, c, d, f, g, l, m, n, p, r, s, t) denotes a voiced (or voiceless for f, s, p, t, c) consonant of the corresponding place and manner. Quality (broad/slender) is determined by neighboring vowels.
- **Gemination** (ll, nn, rr) usually indicates that the consonant is "tense" or long, but in phonemic transcription is often not distinguished from a single one (except in cases where there is a minimal pair).
- **Combinations with ⟨h⟩** (bh, ch, dh, fh, gh, mh, ph, sh, th) denote:
  - Lenition (e.g., ⟨bh⟩ = /w/ or /vʲ/; ⟨fh⟩ = zero; ⟨sh⟩ = /h/).
  - At the end of a word or before a consonant – certain fricatives.
- **Combinations with ⟨f⟩** (bf, cf, df, gf, lf, mf, nf, pf, rf, tf) denote voiceless plosives (for b,d,g – this is devoicing) or voiceless sonorants (for l,m,n,r).
- **Combinations with ⟨t⟩** (bth, cht, dth, gth, lth, mth, nth, pth, rth, tth) often denote voiceless plosives in past participle endings (-tha, -the).

## 7.3 Vowel Graphemes (Graiféimí gutacha)

### Principles

- Irish orthography distinguishes **broad** (⟨a, á, o, ó, u, ú⟩) and **slender** (⟨e, é, i, í⟩) vowels.
- Vowel letters can be single (short or long) and diphthongs (vowel combinations, sometimes with ⟨i⟩ or ⟨h⟩ to denote palatalisation of neighboring consonants).
- Important: **the letter ⟨i⟩ often functions as a "palataliser"** – it is not always pronounced as /ɪ/ or /iː/, but may merely indicate the slenderness of neighboring consonants (e.g., at the end of a word *faoi* – /iː/ at the end, but in the middle *cairde* – /a/).
- **Length** is marked by an acute accent (á, é, í, ó, ú) or in some combinations (⟨ao⟩, ⟨ae⟩ etc.).

## Key Takeaways for the G2P Script from Chapter 7

1. **Determining consonant quality:** look at neighboring vowel letters:
   - If there is at least one slender vowel (⟨e, é, i, í⟩) and no broad vowel (⟨a, á, o, ó, u, ú⟩) in the same syllable or in immediate proximity – the consonant is slender (palatalized).
   - Otherwise – broad (velarized).
   - Exceptions: ⟨s⟩ before ⟨f, m, p⟩ – always broad; ⟨r⟩ at the beginning or before ⟨d, l, n, t, s⟩ – always broad.

2. **Consonant graphemes** give the base phoneme, and quality (broad/slender) is added based on neighboring vowels. Voiceless variants are obtained from combinations with ⟨f⟩, ⟨th⟩, ⟨sh⟩.

3. **Vowel letters** can indicate:
   - The vowel sound itself (short or long) – tables 7.14–7.27.
   - The quality of neighboring consonants (especially ⟨i⟩ at the end of a word or before another vowel – often silent, only an indicator of slenderness).
   - A diphthong (combinations of vowels with ⟨i⟩, ⟨a⟩, ⟨u⟩, and ⟨dh⟩, ⟨gh⟩, ⟨mh⟩, ⟨bh⟩ often give diphthongs /ai/, /au/, /iə/, /uə/).

4. **Dialectal differences** are critical:
   - GD: ⟨ao⟩ = /iː/, ⟨ae⟩ = /eː/, /æː/ for ⟨á⟩ in some positions, diphthong /ia/.
   - CR: ⟨ao⟩ = /iː/, /aː/ for ⟨á⟩, diphthongs /ai/, /au/, /iə/, /uə/.
   - CD: ⟨ao⟩ = /iː/, /ɑː/ for ⟨á⟩, /ʌ/ widespread, diphthong /əu/, /iə/ for ⟨éa⟩.

5. **Morphological opacity** (initial mutations, endings) requires a separate module: mutations change the initial consonant (e.g., ⟨b⟩ → ⟨bh⟩ = /w/ or /vʲ/, ⟨c⟩ → ⟨ch⟩ = /x/ or /ç/, etc.). In G2P, mutation rules should be applied first (if the input is a full word in context), or work with the base form and handle mutations separately.

6. **Tables 7.1–7.13 and 7.14–7.27** are essentially a **complete G2P dictionary** for Irish in three dialects. For a G2P script, these rules should be implemented as:
   - Contextual replacements (Cʷ/Cʲ) based on environment.
   - Special rules for graphemes involving ⟨h⟩, ⟨f⟩, ⟨t⟩ to denote voiceless and lenited forms.
   - A separate set of rules for vowels and diphthongs taking the dialect into account.
