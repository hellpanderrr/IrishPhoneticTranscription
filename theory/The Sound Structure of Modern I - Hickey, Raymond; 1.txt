Below is a detailed summary of the first chapter of Raymond Hickey's "The Sound Structure of Modern Irish" (2014). The chapter is titled "Introduction" and covers historical context, the current state of the language, dialect divisions, and main research directions. It serves as an introduction to the subsequent phonological and morphophonological analysis.

1. Historical background
The Irish language belongs to the Celtic branch of the Indo-European family. Together with Scottish Gaelic and Manx, it forms the Q-Celtic subgroup (which preserved the original *kʷ), whereas Welsh, Cornish, and Breton belong to the P-Celtic group (where *kʷ became p).
Proto-Celtic existed on the European continent in the 1st millennium BC (Hallstatt and La Tène cultures). Speakers of Q-Celtic migrated to Ireland roughly between 500 and 300 BC. A characteristic feature of the Celtic languages is the loss of initial *p (cf. Latin pisces → Irish iasc).

1.1. Ogham period (400–600 AD)
The earliest written monuments are Ogham inscriptions (5th–6th centuries), carved on stone (mainly in the south and southwest of Ireland). These are mostly names in the genitive case. Already in this period, weakening of intervocalic consonants (lenition) was observed, and long (geminated) consonants existed.

1.2. Old Irish (600–900)
The period begins with the Christianization of Ireland (5th century). The earliest manuscripts (glosses) date from the mid-8th century (in Germany, Switzerland, and Northern Italy). The language is characterized by:

lenition (already in intervocalic position, attested in Latin and Scandinavian borrowings, e.g. lebor → leabhar "book");

"vowel coloring" (umlaut) under the influence of the following syllable, but unlike in Germanic languages, it did not become grammaticalized, because Irish was actively losing endings and shifting to consonantal means of expressing grammatical meanings.

1.3. Middle Irish (900–1200)
Decline of the Classical Old Irish standard, penetration of colloquial forms into writing. Simplification of inflections (loss of distinctions between genitive and dative cases in many nouns), development of independent pronouns (instead of infixed ones). Beginning of dialect fragmentation (not yet reflected in writing). The end of the period — the Norman invasion (1169).

1.4. Classical Modern Irish (1200–1600)
Period of professional poetry (filid). Grammatical tracts for poets (so-called Bardic Syntactical Tracts) are created. There was a gap between the poetic norm (ceart na bhfileadh) and colloquial speech (canamhain).
Phonetically: loss of Interdental /θ/ and /ð/ (13th century), which gave different outcomes for lenition: t → h, d → t. The system of verbal prefixes simplifies (in particular, *do-* becomes a marker of the past tense).

1.5. Early Modern Irish (1600–1900)
Fall of the Gaelic aristocracy and the system of patronage of poets (beginning of the 17th century) → decline of the written standard. Intensification of English influence, enactment of the Penal Laws against Catholics → beginning of mass shift to English.
The Great Famine (1845–1848) and emigration sharply reduced the number of speakers. By the end of the 19th century, Irish-speaking areas (Gaeltacht) were fragmented into three large regions: Cork-Kerry, Galway-Mayo, and Donegal (plus a small enclave in Waterford).

1.6. Modern Irish (1900– )
After the independence of southern Ireland (1922), Irish received the status of the first official language (Constitution of 1937). However, in reality less than 1% of the population uses it as a native language (about tens of thousands of people). Most Irish people know the language only passively or symbolically.
Since 2007, Irish has been an official language of the European Union. The prospects of the language depend on its perception as a functional medium in modern society.

2. Dialects of Modern Irish
Traditionally, three main dialect zones are distinguished:

Northern (County Donegal),

Western (County Galway — Connemara),

Southern (Counties Kerry, Cork, Waterford).

Originally there was a dialect continuum along the western and southern coasts, but with the fragmentation of areas, three separate blocks formed.
The official name for Irish-speaking areas is Gaeltacht (subdivided into Fíor-Ghaeltacht — with a high percentage of speakers, and Breac-Ghaeltacht — with fewer speakers).

2.1. Dialect studies
The key work is Heinrich Wagner's dialectological atlas (1958–64). The main monographic descriptions (1940–1960s) were produced under the auspices of the Dublin Institute for Advanced Studies. These are the works of de Bhaldraithe (Connemara), Breatnach (Rinn, Waterford), de Búrca (Tourmakeady, Mayo), Ó Cuív (Muskerry, Cork), Mhac an Fhailigh (Erris, Mayo), and others.
Later studies filled the gaps: Stockman (Achill), Hamilton (Tory), Lucas (Rossgier). The modern multi-volume description of Irish, Iorras Aithneach (Connemara) — by Ó Curnáin (2007).

2.2. Linguistic studies of Irish phonology
Major dissertations and monographs from the second half of the 20th to the early 21st century:

in the framework of generative phonology (Boyle / Ó Baoill, Wigger, Kelly, Ní Chiosáin, Ní Shiadhail, Cyran, Bloch-Rozme);

descriptive approach (Ó Siadhail and Wigger, 1975; Ó Siadhail, 1989).
The latter has been criticized for attempting to postulate abstract underlying forms common to all dialects (a legacy of generative phonology).

2.3. Main phonetic differences between dialects
Systematic tables of differences in vowel and consonant realization are provided. For example:

Vowels
<AO> (long front with retraction): in the north [iː] (sometimes [ɯː]), in the west [iː], in the south [eː].

Length before former geminated sonorants: in the north, shortness is preserved (am [am]), in the west, length (am [aːm]), in the south, diphthong (am [aum]).

Results of fricative vocalization (agh, abh, amh): in the north [eː, oː, oː], in the west [ai, au, oː], in the south [ai, au, au].

Stress: in the north — on the first syllable (with shortening of post-tonic long vowels), in the west — predominantly initial, in the south — attracted to a long vowel of a non-first syllable (cailín [kaˈlʲiːnʲ]).

Consonants
Palatal coronals at the beginning of a stressed syllable (<t-, d->): in the north, affricates [tʃ, dʒ], in the west, palatal stops [tʲ, dʲ], in the south, alveolar [tʲ, dʲ] (weak palatalization).

/x/ in initial position: north [h], west [x], south [x]; in final position: north [Ø], west [x], south [x].

Intervocalic /h/ after a stressed vowel: in the north it is preserved, in the west it disappears in some areas (Cois Fharraige), in the south it is preserved.

Realization of /v/ before a non-palatal vowel: in the north and west [w], in the south [v].

Prosody
Stress — described separately (see also section II.3).

2.4. Data from lost dialect areas
Apart from the modern Gaeltacht, there are recordings from the early 20th century (Wilhelm Dögen collection, 1928–1931), restored and digitized. They contain the last speakers from counties Antrim, Derry, Tyrone, Cavan, Armagh, Louth, Sligo, Roscommon, Clare, Tipperary. These recordings provide insight into the phonology of extinct dialects.

2.5. Written documentation of dialects
An extensive bibliography for individual regions (counties and localities) is provided, grouped by province:

South: Kilkenny, Ring (Waterford), Cape Clear, Muskerry, Uíbh Ráthach, Corca Dhuibhne, Limerick.

West: Clare, Aran Islands, Connemara (and its subregions: Cois Fharraige, Ros Muc, Iorras Aithneach, Sruth Sáileach), Mayo (Tourmakeady, Achill, Erris, East Mayo).

North: Donegal (Tír Chonaill, Glenties, Torr) and lost enclaves in Tyrone, Antrim, Rathlin, South Armagh, Cooley (Louth).

For many of these areas, grammatical descriptions, word lists, and texts have been published (e.g. Sommerfelt, Quiggin, Holmer, Lavin, et al.).

Chapter conclusion
The first chapter lays the historical-linguistic and dialectological foundation for the further study of the sound structure of the Irish language. The author emphasizes that the book is oriented toward a synchronic description, but with necessary diachronic explanations. The main focus is on the Connemara (western) dialect as a convenient reference point, which does not, however, have the status of a standard. The chapter also introduces the reader to key research works and data sources (including audio materials on DVD accompanying the author's previous book).

II. The phonological framework
1. Sound inventory
The Irish language has the following key characteristics:

VSO word order, post-specification;

developed initial mutation (lenition and nasalization);

systemic opposition of palatal and non-palatal consonants (except /h/);

voicing distinction for most stops and fricatives;

absence of voiced sibilants (/z/, /ʒ/);

absence of affricates (except dialectal and sandhi phenomena);

absence of phonological consonant length (present in Old Irish, lost);

distinction between long and short vowels.

1.1. Palatal vs. non-palatal
Palatal consonants (denoted [ʲ]) are produced with the raising of the middle part of the tongue toward the palate. Non-palatal (velarized) consonants are produced with the raising of the back part of the tongue toward the soft palate, denoted [ˠ].
Examples:

lá [l̪ˠaː] "day" vs. leá [lʲaː] "melting";

dáil [daːlʲ] "people" vs. dáil [daːlˠ] "assembly" (distinction at the end).

1.1.1. Primary and secondary palatals
Primary (coronal): tʲ, dʲ, sʲ, nʲ, lʲ, rʲ;

Secondary (non-coronal): pʲ, bʲ, fʲ, vʲ, mʲ (labial) and kʲ, gʲ, xʲ, ɣʲ (velar).

1.1.2–1.1.3. Realization of contrast and polarity
Polarity — the degree of deviation of the tongue from neutral position. For coronal nasals and liquids in the western and northern dialects, there is a three-way contrast:

maximal palatality: nʲ, lʲ;

neutral (low polarity): nᵢ, lᵢ (e.g. baile [balʲə] "town");

maximal velarization: nˠ, lˠ (e.g. naomh [nˠiːv] "saint").

In the southern dialect, velarization is weaker, with only a two-way contrast: palatal (nʲ, lʲ) vs. non-palatal (nˠ, lˠ).

Example minimal pair for the western dialect:

buille [bɪlʲə] "blow" (palatal) vs. buile [bɪlᵢə] "fury" (neutral).

1.2. Palatality vs. Palatalization (process)
Palatality — a lexical property (e.g. ní [nʲiː] "washing" vs. naoi [nˠiː] "nine").

Palatalization/depalatalization — a morphophonological process of changing polarity to express a grammatical category (e.g. nominative vs. genitive case: gabhar [gˠəuɾˠ] "goat.NOM" → gabhair [gˠəuɾʲ] "goat.GEN").

1.3. Status of the feature [palatal]
The feature [palatal] can persist even after the deletion of the segment that carried it. Example: an‑fhiach [ənʲˈiəx] "very valuable" — /fʲ/ is elided, but palatality transfers to the preceding /n/ (normally non-palatal).

1.4. Independent and dependent consonants
Independent — occur in initial position in dictionary forms (e.g. /p, b, m, f, s, t, d, k, g/ and their palatal counterparts).

Dependent — appear only as a result of mutation (e.g. /ɣ, x, v/ word-initially, except for rare grammatical words with permanent lenition like chun [xun] "to").

1.5. Pairwise notation
To describe processes that do not distinguish palatality, a single letter is used to denote a pair (e.g. T = /t/ and /tʲ/). This is convenient for mutations: lenition T → /h/ regardless of palatality.

Examples of pairs: P (p, pʲ), B (b, bʲ), M (m, mʲ), F (f, fʲ), V (v, vʲ), T (t, tʲ), D (d, dʲ), N (n, nˠ), L (l, lˠ), R (r, rʲ), S (s, sʲ), K (k, kʲ), G (g, gʲ), X (x, xʲ), Y (ɣ, ɣʲ), and also J for the velar nasal [ŋ] (only as a result of mutation).

1.6. Lexical sets
The author introduces word sets for consonant and vowel phonemes (analogous to Wells's system for English). Examples:

Consonants:

P: post [pˠɔsˠt̪ˠ] "job", pʲ: pioc [pʲɪk] "pluck"

T: tóg [t̪ˠoːg] "take", tʲ: teach [tʲax] "house"

S: súil [sˠuːlʲ] "eye", sʲ: siúl [ʃuːlˠ] "walking"

L: lui [l̪ˠiː] "lying", lʲ: léamh [lʲeːv] "reading"

K: cá [kˠaː] "where", kʲ: ceart [kʲaɾˠt̪ˠ] "correct"

X: a charr [ə xaːɾˠ] "his car", xʲ: a cheann [ə çan̪ˠ] "his head"

Y: dhá [ɣaː] "two", ɣʲ: dhíol [jiːlˠ] "sold"

Vowels: short /ɪ, e, a, ʌ, ə/, long /iː, eː, aː, oː, uː/, diphthongs /ai, au, iə, uə/.

1.7. Consonant system
1.7.1. Stops
Seven pairs, of which six are obstruent and one is sonorant (/m/ behaves as a stop rather than a sonorant for several reasons).
The voicing distinction is phonetically stable.

Labial stops (P, B):

Non-palatal have a velar glide [w] before vowels: bui [bˠwiː] "yellow".

Palatal — with lip tension and a brief [j]-like transition: beo [bʲoː] "alive".

In the western dialect, epenthetic /b/ at the end of prepositions: acu [akəbˠ] "at them".

Coronal stops (T, D):

Non-palatal — dental stops.

Palatal — in the northern dialect affricates [tʃ, dʒ], in the western pure palatals [tʲ, dʲ], in the southern alveolar with slight palatalization.

Sandhi-affricate [tʃ] arises from the sequence /x/ + /sʲ/ (e.g. bhiodh sé [vʲiːx ʃeː] → [vʲiːtʃeː] "he would be").

Velar stops (K, G):

Palatal — shifted forward: ceart [kʲaɾˠt̪ˠ] "correct".

In the western and northern dialects, final palatal velars in unstressed syllables are devoiced: Nollaig [ˈn̪ˠɔl̪ˠəkʲ] "Christmas".

In the southern dialect, historical /-ij/ closes to /-igʲ/ (e.g. traigh → tráig [t̪ˠɾˠaːigʲ] "beach").

Loanwords from English have been adapted with voicing of final stops: cnag [knˠag] "knock" (from Eng. knock).

1.7.2. Fricatives
Five pairs: F, V, S, X, Y, plus /h/ outside the opposition. Voiced fricatives /v, ɣ/ are dependent phonemes (except in intervocalic position). Voiced sibilants are absent (neither /z/ nor /ʒ/).

Labial fricatives (F, V):

Non-palatal /v/ in the western and northern dialects weakens to [β] or [w] in intervocalic position and initially: a bhua [ə wuə] "his victory".

Word-finally /v/ can vocalize: garbh [ˈgˠaɾˠəv] ~ [ˈgˠaɾˠuː] "rough".

In the southern dialect, palatal /vʲ/ often vocalizes: sliabh [ʃlʲiəv] "mountain" → gen. sleibhe [ʃlʲeː] (vocalization).

Sibilants (S):

Only voiceless: /s/ (non-palatal, velarized before front vowels) and /sʲ/ ([ʃ]) — palatal.

Lenition S → /h/ (e.g. shnámh sé [hnˠaːv ʃeː] "he swam").

Before palatal labials (mʲ, pʲ), palatalization of /s/ does not occur: sméar [sʲmʲeːɾˠ] "berry", not *[ʃmʲeːɾˠ].

Velar fricatives (X, Y):

/x/ (non-palatal), /xʲ/ ([ç]) — dependent initially (except chun, chomh).

In the northern dialect, /x/ disappears word-finally, before /t/ it is realized as [ɾˠ]: slacht [sˠl̪ˠaɾˠt̪ˠ] "neatness".

/ɣ/ (non-palatal) and /ɣʲ/ ([j]) — always a result of mutation. In the northern dialect, non-palatal /ɣ/ often fronts to [j]: dhá [jaː] "two".

Glottal fricative /h/:

Occurs as a result of lenition of T and S, and also as a marker of the feminine gender possessive adjective: a hainm [ə hanʲəmʲ] "her name".

Between vowels it can be elided: oíche [iː] "night" (in Cois Fharraige).

In southwestern Connemara /h/ is preserved and shortens the preceding vowel.

1.7.3. Shifts in articulation
Related to lenition: /x/ → /h/ (in the northern dialect, cha [ha] "not").

Related to fortition: /h/ → /x/ (e.g. ó shin [oː xinʲ] "since then").

Shift of place of articulation: /x/ → /f/ in some words (e.g. cluiche [klˠɪfʲə] "game" in some dialects).

Shift of /θ/ (historical) → /x/ in the southern dialect: sruthán [sˠɾˠʊˈxɑːnˠ] "stream".

1.8. Sonorant system
Includes nasals (m, n, ŋ) and liquids (l, r).
Characteristics of /m/: behaves as a stop (lenition to /v/, does not allow lenition of S before itself, blocks prefixation of T).

Three-way contrast for coronal nasals and laterals (in the western and northern dialects):

palatal [nʲ, lʲ] — high polarity;

neutral [nᵢ, lᵢ] — low polarity (after a short stressed vowel, e.g. duine [d̪ˠɪnᵢə] "person");

velarized [nˠ, lˠ] — high velarity (e.g. naoi [nˠiː] "nine").

Examples of minimal pairs: ceile [ˈkʲelʲə] "spouse" vs. ceille [ˈkʲel̠ʲə] "sense.GEN" (different degree of palatality).

R-sounds:

Non-palatal /r/ — usually an approximant [ɹ] or [ɾ], may be uvular [ʀ] (in some dialects, especially among older speakers).

Palatal /rʲ/ — can yield affricate-like [ʒ] after palatal labials: breá [bʲʒaː] "fine".

Word-initially the opposition by palatality is neutralized — palatal /rʲ/ does not occur initially. In clusters after lenition it depalatalizes: an fhreagra [ənʲˈɾˠaɡɾˠə] "the answer" (from /fʲɾʲaɡɾˠə/).

Historical development:
In Old Irish there were geminate sonorants. Upon their simplification in Middle Irish, the preceding vowel lengthened (in the west/north) or became a diphthong (in the south).

West: am [aːmˠ] "time" vs. ama [amˠə] "time.GEN".

South: am [aumˠ] vs. ama [amˠə].

North: shortness is preserved — am [amˠ].

Modern diphthongs /ai/ and /au/ (south, west) often alternate with short vowels in paradigms: poll [pauɫ̪] "hole.NOM" vs. poill [paiʎ̟] "hole.GEN".

Phonological analysis:
The author rejects abstract solutions (geminates in underlying representation) in favor of lexical tagging: words with sonorants that cause vowel lengthening in monosyllables are marked as [+long] ← [+son]. This is not predictable from the phonetic form.

1.9. Vowel system
Four short stressed vowels (/ɪ, e, a, ʌ/), five long (/iː, eː, aː, oː, uː/), four diphthongs (/ai, au, iə, uə/). Unstressed short vowels reduce to [ə] and [ɪ] depending on the palatality of neighboring consonants.

Long vowels:
Stable, but there are dialectal shifts:

North: /aː/ → [æː] or [eː], /uː/ → [ʉː], /oː/ → [ɔː] (lowering).

West: /eː/ sometimes diphthongizes to /ai/ (déan [dʲaːnˠ] "to do" ~ [dʲiːnˠ] in rapid speech).

Short vowels:

/a/ after a palatal → [æ] (or [æː] in Cois Fharraige): teach [tʲæːx] "house".

/ʌ/ (historically < *u and *o) — in the western and southern dialects pronounced as [ʌ] (unrounded mid-low back). Before velar stops it raises to [ʊ]: muc [mˠʊk] "pig".

Before /r/ + consonant, /a/ lengthens and backs: carr [kaːɾˠ] "car".

Vowels before former geminated sonorants:
Table of differences:

ill: north [ɪl̠ʲ], west [ɪl̠ʲ], south [iːlʲ] (but the example maoil "delay": north [iːlʲ], west [iːlʲ], south [eːlʲ] — depends on the word).

am: north [amˠ], west [aːmˠ], south [aumˠ].

poll: north [pˠɔl̪ˠ] or [pˠʌl̪ˠ], west [pˠauɫ̪], south [pˠəul̪ˠ].

Diphthongs:

/ai/ arises from old sequences -adh, -agh (e.g. radharc [ɾˠaɾˠk] "vision") and before palatal former geminates (poill [pˠaiʎ̟]).

/au/ from -abh, -amh, -odh, -ogh (leabhar [lʲauɾˠ] "book", samhradh [sˠauɾˠə] "summer").

/iə/ and /uə/ — centering diphthongs, complementary distribution: /iə/ only after palatals, /uə/ after non-palatals: bía [bʲiə] "food" vs. bua [bˠuə] "victory".

Vocalization of fricatives:
Historical /ɣ/ and /v/ in postvocalic position turned into vowels:

-abh, -amh → /au/ (cabhair [kəuɾʲ] "help").

-adh, -agh → /ai/ (aghaidh [əi] "face").

In the northern dialect often simply a long monophthong: leabhar [lʲoːɾˠ].

Unstressed vowels:
Only [ə] and [ɪ] (after palatals). No contrast. In the northern dialect, final unstressed /ə/ is often replaced by [iː]: teanga [tʲæŋgiː] "tongue" (in the west [tʲæŋgə]).

1.10. The syllable
Irish belongs to languages with a syllable weight contrast. Syllabic sonority rises toward the nucleus and falls toward the final coda. The maximum number of elements initially is three (S + stop + liquid), finally two (sonorant or fricative + stop). Sequences of two fricatives, two stops, and two sonorants are prohibited (in simple words). Example of a permissible structure: bocht [bˠʌxt̪ˠ] "poor" (fricative + stop in the coda).

2. Phonotactics
2.1. Permissible clusters
Initial two-member: combinations of fricative (only S) + stop (sp-, st-, sc-) are allowed, as well as stop + sonorant (kn-, gn-, mn-, ml-, mr-).

Initial three-member: S + stop + liquid (spr, str, scr, spl, scl; stl is absent).

Final: sonorant or fricative + stop. Combinations like ‑nd, -ld have historically simplified.

Prohibited: two fricatives in a row (even across morpheme boundaries), two stops, two sonorants.

Peculiarity of /x/: it does not assimilate in palatality to the following consonant (unlike others). For example, bocht [bˠʌxt̪ˠ] → boichte [bˠʌxtʲə] "poorer" (no change of /x/ → /ç/).

2.2–2.3. Cluster simplification and sonorant shift
Simplification: loss of a sonorant in stop+sonorant combinations: Padraig [pˠaːɾˠəc] "Patrick", codladh [kʌl̪ˠə] "sleep".

Shift n → r in clusters: mná [mˠɾˠaː] "women", cnoc [kɾˠʌk] "hill".

In loanwords: knitting → cniotáil [kʲnʲɪt̪ˠaːlʲ] or [kʲlʲɪt̪ˠaːlʲ] (shift n → l).

2.4. Interpretation of /h/
/h/ can have a palatal allophone [ç] before a palatal vowel: a Sheáin [ə çaːnʲ] "John (voc.)". Before sonorants, /h/ devoices them: a shrón [ə hɾˠoːnˠ] → [ə ɾ̥ˠoːnˠ] "his nose".

2.5. Vowel phonotactics
All vowels can occur word-initially and word-finally. Long vowels are frequent in final position: áit [aːtʲ] "place", bó [bˠoː] "cow". Short vowels word-finally only in monosyllables: te [tʲe] "hot".
High frequency of /iː/ in final position as a marker of: comparative (suntasach → suntasaí), verbal adjective (tógtha → [t̪ˠoːkiː] "taken"), plural (amanna → amannaí "times").

2.6. Prefixed consonants
Prefix F: restoration of lost /f/ in some words: uar → fuar "cold".

Prefix P: leadhb → pleadhb [pʲlʲəuβ] "clumsy person".

Prefix T: metanalysis of the article: an t‑aiséadach → taiséadach "shroud".

Prefix S: expressive or onomatopoeic meaning: scabhaitéir "scoundrel", slabóg "sloven".

2.7. Minor processes
Internal lenition and voicing: intervocalic /s/ does not voice (unlike other fricatives).

Final devoicing: only for palatal velars in unstressed syllables.

Assimilation of /h/ to the following vowel in palatality.

Depalatalization of /r/ syllable-finally before a stop: ceird [kʲaɾˠdʲ] "trade".

Assimilation across word boundaries (e.g. palatalization of the article an before a word with a historical front vowel: an iobairt [ə ˈnʲiːbˠəɾˠtʲ] "sacrifice").

Elision: loss of /f/ in uafásach [uːəsˠəx] "terrible", loss of /x/ in the northern dialect.

2.8. Epenthesis
Occurs in heavy codas consisting of two sonorants or sonorant + obstruent (except combinations with /r/ as the first element).

Rule: in a sequence "sonorant + sonorant" or "sonorant + obstruent", a vowel is inserted that copies the polarity of the cluster.

Examples:

bolg [ˈbˠʌlˠəg] "belly" (underlying /bˠʌlg/);

garbh [ˈgˠaɾˠəv] "rough" (underlying /gˠaɾˠv/).

III. The morphonology of Irish
1. Origins and development of initial mutation
1.1. Division within the Celtic languages
The insular Celtic languages (Irish, Scottish Gaelic, Manx, Welsh, Cornish, Breton) developed a unique system of initial mutations, absent in Continental Celtic (Gaulish, etc.).
Subgroups:

Q-Celtic (Irish, Scottish Gaelic, Manx) — preserve PIE *kʷ as /k/ or /kʲ/.

P-Celtic (Welsh, Cornish, Breton) — *kʷ → /p/.

1.2. Primitive Irish: Ogham inscriptions (400–600 AD)
In Ogham inscriptions, intervocalic weakening of stops (phonetic lenition) is already observed, but grammaticalized mutations are not yet present. Examples: lebor "book" (from Latin liber) shows voicing of intervocalic /b/ → /v/. In this period, geminates (long consonants) also existed.

1.3. Mutations in Old Irish (600–900)
In Old Irish, three types of mutations are attested:

Lenition: stops become fricatives in intervocalic position or after a vowel. Outcomes: /p/ → /f/, /t/ → /θ/, /k/ → /x/, /b/ → /β/, /d/ → /ð/, /g/ → /ɣ/, /s/ → /h/, /f/ → /∅/ (later).

Nasalisation: occurs after nasal particles (e.g. in "in"). Voiced stops become nasals: /b/ → /m/, /d/ → /n/, /g/ → /ŋ/; voiceless stops become voiced and may become nasalized (later simplified).

Aspiration (prefixation of /h/) — develops later, marks the feminine gender of possessive adjectives and some other contexts.

1.4. Evidence of lenition in Celtic
Lenition arises from a sandhi phenomenon: intervocalic voicing and weakening in connected speech after vowels. In Irish, it becomes grammatical after certain particles, prepositions, and possessive adjectives.

Examples from borrowings:

Latin liber → Old Irish lebor → Modern leabhar /lʲauɾˠ/ "book": intervocalic /b/ weakened to /v/, then lost with compensatory lengthening (south/west — diphthong /au/, north — long /oː/).

Scandinavian markaðr → Irish margadh /ˈmˠaɾˠəgə/ "market": weakening of /g/ after a vowel.

Anglo-Norman bacun → Irish bagún /bˠəˈguːnˠ/ "bacon": intervocalic /k/ → /g/ (voicing).

1.5. Origin of mutation in Celtic
Mutations arose from phonetic sandhi processes in the prehistoric period, then were reinterpreted by children acquiring the language as grammatical markers. Stages:

Intervocalic voicing and weakening (lenition) after vowels.

Assimilation in nasality after particles with a final nasal (e.g. in).

Grammaticalization: mutations become obligatory in certain syntactic environments, even if the phonetic conditions have disappeared (e.g. after the loss of a final vowel or nasal).

1.5.1. Relative chronology of mutations
Lenition appears earlier than nasalization (already in Ogham inscriptions). Nasalization develops from assimilation with a final nasal of a preposition or particle (e.g. in). In Old Irish, both systems are fully functional.

1.5.2. Example: 3rd person possessive adjectives
*a* "his" (triggers lenition): a charr /ə xaːɾˠ/ "his car" (k → x).

*a* "her" (triggers h-prefixation before a vowel): a hainm /ə hanʲəmʲ/ "her name".

*a* "their" (triggers nasalization): a gcarr /ə gaːɾˠ/ (k → g) or /ə ŋaːɾˠ/ depending on dialect; a mbád /ə mˠaːd̪ˠ/ "their boat" (b → m).

1.6. Minimal mutation system
For Modern Irish, the minimal system includes:

Lenition (secular shift): p → f (ph), b → v (bh), m → v (mh), t → h (th), d → ɣ (dh) → [ɣ, j], k → x (ch), g → ɣ (gh), s → h (sh), f → 0 (fh).

Nasalisation: p → b, t → d, k → g; b → m, d → n, g → ŋ; f → v (in some dialects).

H-prefixation (before vowels after feminine *a* and after the article na in plural): a hiníon /ə hɪnʲiːnˠ/ "her daughter".

T-prefixation (before vowels after the article an in masculine singular): an t‑athair /ən̪ˠ t̪ˠahəɾʲ/ "the father".

1.6.1. Regularity and scope of mutations
Mutations are regular and obligatory in grammatical contexts, but may have lexical exceptions. For example, lenition after an "very" does not apply to all words: an‑mhór "very big" (lenition), but an‑maith "very good" (without lenition, since the initial /m/ does not lenite after an).

1.6.2. System violations
In the northern dialect (Donegal), nasalization after *a* "their" is often replaced by lenition or zero: a gcarr is pronounced as [ə xaːɾˠ] (lenition) or [ə kaːɾˠ] (without mutation).

1.6.3. Overlap and zero in mutations
Some mutations lead to identical results: lenition t → h and s → h give /h/ in both cases. This leads to homophony in some forms, e.g. a thí "his house" (t → h) and a shí "her/their fairy tale" (s → h) — are pronounced identically [ə hiː].

1.7. Effectiveness of mutation systems
The system is highly functional: it marks syntactic relations (e.g. subject after a verb in the past tense), distinguishes possessive adjectives, case forms in noun phrases, tense and mood markers of verbs.

1.7.1. Analogical spread and regularity
Analogy spreads mutations to new words and contexts. For example, lenition after the negative particle of the past tense ní came to be applied to all verbs, even those that historically should not have had it.

1.7.2. Lenition of sonorants
In Old Irish, sonorants /n, l, r/ could also be lenited, but the result was often phonetically weak (neutralization of palatality/velarization). In the modern language, lenition of sonorants is preserved mainly in the northern dialect: n → n (neutral), l → l, r → r without change in place of articulation, but with loss of polarity.
Example: nigh /nʲiː/ "wash (present)" → past tense nigh /n̠ʲiː/ (neutral palatal nasal).

1.7.3. Restructuring of oppositions
As a result of lenition, some phonemes disappeared from initial position (e.g. /ð, θ/ were lost in the Middle Irish period, around the 13th century). Their place was taken by new allophones (/h/ from t and s, /ɣ/ from d and g).

1.7.4. Tension, gemination, and lenition
Historical geminates (doubled consonants) were insensitive to lenition: the intervocalic position did not weaken them. This distinction is preserved in that geminates gave long vowels or diphthongs in modern dialects, while single consonants in intervocalic position weakened and were often lost.

1.8. Extensions after functionalization
After mutations became grammatical, they spread to new contexts: for example, after the numeral dhá "two" (triggers lenition), after the preposition le "with" (in some dialects triggers lenition instead of nasalization).

1.8.1. Redundancy in mutation
In some contexts, mutation is redundant (e.g. after *a* "his" the case is already clear from context), but the system is preserved due to tradition and low functional load.

1.8.2. Analytic tendencies in Irish
Modern Irish shows a tendency toward analyticity: synthetic verb forms are being replaced by verb + independent pronoun combinations (bhris mé "I broke" instead of bhris). Mutations remain, but their functional load decreases.

2.2.1. H-prefix

2.2.2. T-prefix
Appears:

After an (masculine singular article) before a vowel: an t‑athair /ən̪ˠ t̪ˠahəɾʲ/ "the father".

After an (feminine singular in dative, residual in some dialects): ar an t‑sráid /ɛɾˠ ən̪ˠ t̪ˠɾˠaːdʲ/ "on the street" (occurs in the southern dialect).

2.3.1. Nasalisation
Triggers: *a* "their", *i* "in", le "with" (in some contexts), numerals seacht "seven", ocht "eight", naoi "nine", deich "ten", céad "hundred", etc.

Phonetic changes:

Voiceless stops → voiced stops: /p/ → /b/, /t/ → /d/, /k/ → /g/.

Voiced stops → nasal stops: /b/ → /m/, /d/ → /n/, /g/ → /ŋ/.

/f/ → /v/ (in some dialects, e.g. a bhfocal /ə vˠɔkəlˠ/ "their word").

Examples:

2.3.2. Lenition

/pˠ, pʲ/ → /fˠ, fʲ/ (ph)

/bˠ, bʲ/ → /vˠ, vʲ/ (bh)

/mˠ, mʲ/ → /vˠ, vʲ/ (mh)

/t̪ˠ, tʲ/ → /h/ (th)

/d̪ˠ, dʲ/ → /ɣˠ, ɣʲ/ (dh) → [ɣ, j]

/kˠ, kʲ/ → /xˠ, xʲ/ (ch) → [x, ç]

/gˠ, gʲ/ → /ɣˠ, ɣʲ/ (gh) → [ɣ, j]

Examples:

a shúil /ə huːlʲ/ "his eye" (sˠ → h).

3. Organisation and principles
3.1. Base and root
Morphophonological changes affect the edge of the base, not the root. In nouns, palatalization in the genitive case occurs at the end of the base, regardless of the internal structure of the root.

3.2. Type of alteration
Two main types:

Segment replacement (in mutation: /p/ → /f/, /t/ → /h/, etc.).

Change of polarity (palatalization/depalatalization of final consonants of the base).

3.3. Base margin alteration
In inflection, the change affects the final consonant of the base (and possibly the preceding short vowel).
Examples:

Nom. bord /bˠoːɾˠd̪ˠ/ "table" → Gen. boird /bˠoːɾˠdʲ/ (palatalization of /d̪ˠ/ → /dʲ/).

Nom. ceann /kʲaːn̪ˠ/ "head" → Pl. cinn /kʲiːnʲ/ (palatalization of /n̪ˠ/ → /nʲ/ + vowel change /aː/ → /iː/).

Nom. blas /bˠl̪ˠasˠ/ "taste" → Gen. blais /bˠl̠aʃ/ (palatalization of /sˠ/ → /sʲ/ + vowel /a/ → /a/ — in reality /a/ becomes [æ] or [e] depending on dialect).

3.4. Root extension and remnants of older patterns
Some older declension types preserve traces of ancient suffixes (e.g. *-t-* in the genitive case). Example: ainm /anʲəmʲ/ "name.NOM", gen. ainmne /anʲəmʲnʲə/ "name.GEN" (suffix *-n-*).

3.5. Scope of base margin alteration
The change affects only the final segment of the base (including clusters). Internal segments are not affected, except short vowels, which assimilate in palatality to the following consonant (rule: before a palatal consonant, a short vowel becomes front: /a/ → [e], /ʌ/ → [ɪ], etc.).

3.6. Anomalies in the mutation system
Some words do not mutate where expected, especially loanwords or rare lexemes. For example, dúchas "heritage" after an "very" remains without lenition in some dialects: an‑dúchas (instead of expected an‑dhúchas).

3.7. Canonical word form
The canonical word in Irish has stress on the first syllable (except the southern dialect), consists of 2–3 syllables, contains an initial consonant that can mutate. The final consonant often determines the grammatical category (palatal vs. non-palatal marker of case/number).

4. Origins and development of palatalisation
4.1. Spread of palatalisation
Palatalisation arose in Old Irish in the environment of front vowels /i, e/ and the semi-vowel /j/ (e.g. in endings). Later it spread to all consonants, including labials, by analogy with coronals and velars, to create a symmetrical system of pairs.

4.2. Contrast in lexical words
The palatal vs. non-palatal contrast is phonological and serves to distinguish lexemes:

bó /bˠoː/ "cow" vs. beo /bʲoː/ "alive".

mála /mˠaːl̪ˠə/ "bag" vs. meall /mʲaːl̪ˠ/ "to pour".

tá /t̪ˠaː/ "is (verb)" vs. te /tʲe/ "hot".

4.3. Palatalisation and morphonology
Palatalisation is used as a morphophonological process to express grammatical categories (genitive case, plural, comparative degree of adjectives).
Example:

trom /t̪ˠɾˠuːmˠ/ "heavy (positive degree)" → comp. troime /t̪ˠɾˠɪmʲə/ "heavier" (palatalization of final /mˠ/ → /mʲ/ + vowel change /uː/ → /ɪ/).

4.4. Palatalisation in the rhyme of the syllable
Palatalisation always affects the coda of the syllable (final consonant). Initial consonants do not change polarity in inflection (except for initial mutations, which are a separate process). Thus the opposition: pionta /pʲiːn̪ˠt̪ˠə/ "pint" (initial palatal) vs. baint /bˠaːn̠ʲtʲ/ "harvesting" (final palatal).

5. Palatalisation in Russian and Irish
The author conducts a systematic comparison of the two languages with a phonological opposition of palatal and non-palatal consonants. The comparison is important for the typological characterization of Irish.

5.1. Palatalisation: origin, scope, and function
Irish: palatalisation arose from assimilation to front vowels /i, e/ and /j/ in the Old Irish period. Later it spread to all consonants, including labials (by analogy). Functions: lexical (word distinction) and grammatical (marker of case, number, degree of comparison).

Russian: palatalisation (softening) arose from Common Slavic palatal vowels and /j/, but encompassed all consonants except some historically hard ones (ц, ш, ж). Functions: primarily lexical (e.g. брат /brat/ vs. брать /bratʲ/), but also grammatical (hard vs. soft declension variant: стол /stol/ vs. конь /konʲ/; alternations in verbs: любить /lʲʉbʲitʲ/ → люблю /lʲʉblʲʉ/).

5.2. Palatalisation as a "cover feature"
In both languages, the feature [palatal] is independent and can combine with other features (place, manner, voicing). This allows describing mutations and alternations in terms of changing the value of [palatal].

5.3. Position of /j/
Irish: no phoneme /j/. Palatal consonants are independent phonemes. The sound [j] appears only as a realization of /ɣʲ/ (lenition of /gʲ/ or /dʲ/) or as a glide before vowels in some positions.

Russian: the phoneme /j/ exists (iot), and it participates in the formation of palatalized consonants, but is not identical to them (cf. /lʲ/ vs. /j/).

5.4. Segment inventories
5.4.1. Palatalisation of labials
Irish: labials are palatalized (/pʲ, bʲ, mʲ, fʲ, vʲ/) with additional articulation — lip tension and [j]-like transition. Example: beo /bʲoː/ → [bʲoː] "alive".

Russian: labials also have soft pairs (/pʲ, bʲ, mʲ, fʲ, vʲ/), articulation includes advancing the tongue forward. Example: пять /pʲatʲ/.

5.4.2. Palatalisation of velars
Irish: velars are palatalized to front palatals (/kʲ, gʲ, xʲ, ɣʲ/). Example: ceann /kʲaːn̪ˠ/.

Russian: velars /k, g, x/ have soft pairs [kʲ, gʲ, xʲ] (e.g. кино [kʲɪˈno], гид [gʲit], хитрый [xʲitrɨj]).

5.4.3. Interpretation of [i] and [ɨ]
Irish: no phoneme /ɨ/. The vowels [i] and [iː] are front. After non-palatal (velarized) consonants they are phonetically retracted ([i̠]), but this is not phonological.

Russian: there is a phonological opposition of /i/ (after soft) and /ɨ/ (after hard), which reflects the differing degree of palatality of preceding consonants.

5.4.4. Coronal consonants
In both languages, coronals (/t, d, s, n, l, r/) have the most prominent opposition in palatality.

Irish: palatal coronals are apical with raised tongue body; non-palatals are velarized, with lowered tongue body. In the northern dialect, palatal /tʲ, dʲ/ are affricates [tʃ, dʒ].

Russian: front-lingual palatalized — with raising of the middle part of the tongue toward the palate, non-palatalized — with flat or retracted tongue position. No affrication.

5.4.5. Coronal sonorants (/n, l, r/)
Irish: three-way contrast (/nʲ, nᵢ, nˠ/; /lʲ, lᵢ, lˠ/) in the western and northern dialects. In the south — two-way (/nʲ, nˠ/; /lʲ, lˠ/).

Russian: two-way contrast (/n, nʲ/; /l, lʲ/). Russian /r/ has a hard/soft pair (/r, rʲ/); in Irish, /r/ and /rʲ/ are distinct, although /rʲ/ is limited in distribution (does not occur in absolute word-initial position).

5.4.6. Labialisation of vowels
Irish: no front rounded vowels (/y, ø/ are absent). The author links this to the fact that palatal consonants require spread lips, which is incompatible with rounding.

Russian: also lacks phonemic front rounded vowels (/y, ø/ are absent). Rounded /u/ can occur after both hard and soft consonants.

5.4.7. Inflection in Irish and Russian
Irish: palatalisation plays a key role in inflection: nominative vs. genitive case of nouns (bord ~ boird), singular vs. plural (fear ~ fir "man ~ men"), comparative degree of adjectives (mór ~ móra "big ~ big", but comp. níos mó "bigger" — here palatalisation does not participate, an analytic construction is used instead). However, the author notes that in Irish, palatalisation is more grammaticalized than in Russian.

Russian: palatalisation (softness) participates in inflection (hard and soft variants of declension, alternations in verbs), but its role is less systematic than in Irish, since Russian uses more endings and suffixes.

The third chapter examines in detail the morphonology of the Irish language, focusing on two fundamental processes: initial mutations and palatalisation.

Initial mutations (lenition, nasalization, h- and t-prefixation) have a historical origin in sandhi phenomena (intervocalic weakening, assimilation to a nasal), but in the modern language they are purely grammatical. They serve to distinguish possessive adjectives (his/her/their), cases, verb tenses, number, and other categories.

Palatalisation is a systemic phonological opposition for all consonants (except /h/) and simultaneously an active morphophonological process of changing polarity to express grammatical meanings. In the western and northern dialects, coronal sonorants demonstrate a rare three-way contrast (palatal, neutral, velarized).

Comparison with Russian shows typological similarity (presence of palatal/non-palatal pairs), but also differences:

In Irish, palatalisation is more widely used in morphology (especially in nominal declension), in Russian — more in the lexicon.

In Irish, the phoneme /j/ is absent, and labials are palatalized with additional lip tension.

Irish has a three-way sonorant contrast, Russian a two-way one.

Irish preserves initial mutations, which have no analogue in Russian.

The system of mutations in Irish is gradually simplifying (especially in the northern dialect, where nasalization may be replaced by lenition or zero), but remains a crucial characteristic of the language, distinguishing it from other Celtic languages and from most Indo-European ones.

The author emphasizes that a morphophonological approach (combining phonology and morphology) is necessary for an adequate description of Irish, since grammatical categories here are expressed primarily through changes in the sound structure of words (initial mutations, palatalisation, vowel alternation), not only through affixation or word order.

1. Introduction
The aim of the chapter is a typological comparison of the sound structure of Irish with other languages that have similar features. The main focus is on initial mutations (consonantal alternations at word and morpheme boundaries), since this is the most striking feature of Irish morphonology. Compared are the Celtic languages (Scottish Gaelic, Welsh, Breton), some Romance languages (Italian, Spanish), a Germanic language (Danish), Finno-Ugric languages (Finnish, Estonian), as well as isolated or minority languages (Nivkh, Fula, Southern Paiute, languages of Central Vanuatu). Types of mutations, their phonetic realization, grammatical function, and diachronic development are analyzed.

2. Scottish Gaelic
Scottish Gaelic historically derives from northern dialects of Irish, brought to Scotland in the 1st millennium AD. Phonologically it is very close to Irish, but has some differences.

Phonetic features:

Palatal vs. non-palatal consonants are preserved, but realization is different.

The distinction between neutral and velarized sonorants (the three-way contrast of Irish) is absent.

The vowel system is simplified: /iː/ is often retracted to [ɯː] after non-palatals (as in northern Irish). Example: caol "narrow" — [kɯːlˠ] (instead of Ir. [kiːlˠ] or [keːlˠ]).

Stress is almost always on the first syllable.

Initial mutations: the system is almost identical to Irish, but there are differences in the domain of nasalization and lenition:

Lenition: p → ph /f/, t → th /h/, c → ch /x/, b → bh /v/, d → dh /ɣ/, g → gh /ɣ/, m → mh /v/, s → sh /h/, f → fh /∅/. Example: a chat /ə xaʰt̪/ "his cat" (Ir. a chat /ə xat/).

Nasalisation (after mo "my", do "your", *a* "their", preposition an "in"): p → b, t → d, c → g; b → m, d → n, g → ŋ; f → bh /v/ (in some dialects). Example: a bparaidh /ə paraɪ/ → /ə baraɪ/ "their paradise".

H-prefixation: after *a* "her" before a vowel: a h‑athair /ə hahəɾʲ/ "her father".

Difference from Irish: in Gaelic, some lenition contexts are lost or expanded; nasalization after *a* "their" is often replaced by lenition in colloquial speech (as in northern Irish).

3. Welsh
Welsh (P-Celtic) has two mutations: soft (lenition) and nasal (nasalization), and also aspirate mutation — the latter is unique to Welsh.

3.1.1. Nasalisation
Occurs after fy "my", yn "in" (certain contexts), ein "our", and some numerals (tri "three", chwech "six", saith "seven", wyth "eight", naw "nine", deg "ten").

Changes:

/p/ → /m̥/ (voiceless m), /t/ → /n̥/ (voiceless n), /k/ → /ŋ̊/ (voiceless ŋ)

/b/ → /m/, /d/ → /n/, /g/ → /ŋ/

/m/ → /m̥/, /ll/ (voiceless l) → /l/ (voicing), but complex.

Examples: tad /taːd/ "father" → fy nhad /və n̥aːd/ "my father"; cath /kaːθ/ "battle" → fy nghath /və ŋ̊aːθ/ "my battle".

3.1.2. Phonological interpretation of mutations in Welsh
Unlike Irish, where mutants are often independent phonemes, in Welsh mutated consonants often coincide with existing phonemes, but rare segments can appear, e.g. voiceless sonorants (/m̥, n̥, ŋ̊/), which do not occur in other positions.

3.1.3. Two leniting mutations of Welsh
Soft mutation — the most common. Triggers: the article *y* (before feminine nouns in singular after *y*), adjectives after nouns, prepositions am "about", ar "on", at "to", dan "under", drwy "through", gan "with", heb "without", *i* "in", *o* "from", wrth "by", numerals dau "two" (masc.), dwy "two" (fem.), possessive adjectives dy "your" and ei "his", particles *a* (relative), fe (affirmative), mi (northern affirmative), etc.

Aspirate mutation — after *a* "her", ei "her", eu "their", after â "with", gyda "with", cân "song", numerals tri "three" (fem.), chwech "six", saith "seven", wyth "eight", naw "nine", deg "ten". Changes: p → ph /f/, t → th /θ/, c → ch /x/. Example: cath "battle" → ei chath /əi χaːθ/ "her battle". (Aspirate mutation does not affect voiced stops.)

3.1.4. Major and minor lenition
The author distinguishes major lenition — weakening of stops to fricatives (p → f, t → θ, k → x, b → v, d → ð, g → ɣ) and minor lenition, which in Welsh also includes changes of sonorants and voiceless fricatives. In Irish, lenition is more uniform.

3.1.5. Genesis of mutation in Welsh
As in Irish, mutations arose from sandhi phenomena, but Welsh preserved more contexts where the phonetic conditions (intervocalic position, nasality) were originally transparent. For example, soft mutation after dy "your" (historically do) occurred due to the loss of the final vowel that was present in Proto-Celtic. In Irish, analogous changes occurred earlier and became more fully grammaticalized.

4. Breton
Breton — P-Celtic language, brought from Britain to Armorica (modern Brittany) in the 5th–6th centuries. It has four mutations: soft (lenition), aspirate (spirant), hard (mutation, actually fortition), and mixed (mutation). Phonetically, Breton is characterized by the presence of front rounded vowels /y, ø/ (from French influence) and the absence of palatal vs. non-palatal opposition (lost).

Soft mutation:

p → b, t → d, k → g

b → v, d → z (or ð), g → ɣ (→ [x] in some dialects)

m → v, gw → w
Example: kastell /ˈkastɛl/ "castle" → ur gastell /yr ˈgastɛl/ "a castle" (indefinite article fem.).

Aspirate/spirant mutation:

p → f, t → z (or h), k → x

b → v, d → t (rare)
Example: tad /taːd/ "father" → e zad /e zaːd/ "his father" (after *e* "his").

Hard mutation:
Occurs after preverbs and some particles.

b → p, d → t, g → k, gw → kw, m → (rare)
Example: bara /ˈbaːra/ "bread" → a bara /a ˈpara/ (?) — the author does not give a precise example, but indicates fortition.

Mixed mutation:
Combination of soft and aspirate in different contexts, characteristic of the Léon dialect.

Difference from Irish: in Breton, mutations are less predictable and more lexicalized, especially the hard mutation. Moreover, Breton has no palatalization of consonants, which radically distinguishes it from Irish. Nasalization as a separate type of mutation is absent in Breton (preserved only in residual forms).

5. Italian
Italian does not have initial mutations in the Celtic sense, but it has consonant alternation in the Tuscan dialect (and in Standard Italian, based on Tuscan). This is the so-called "gorgia toscana" (Tuscan throat spirantization).

Gorgia toscana:
In intervocalic position (within a word and across word boundaries), voiceless stops /p, t, k/ become fricatives:

/p/ → [ɸ] or [h]

/t/ → [θ]

/k/ → [h] (often) or [x]

Examples:

la casa /la ˈkaːsa/ → [la ˈhaːsa] "the house" (definite article + noun).

il pane /il ˈpaːne/ → [il ˈɸaːne] "the bread" (but after a consonant, no spirantization).

Unlike Irish mutations, gorgia has no grammatical function (purely phonetic sandhi), but is typologically interesting as a similar weakening of stops in a certain position.

6. Spanish
Spanish in the standard Castilian variety does not have initial mutations. However, in the Andalusian and Canarian dialects, processes reminiscent of mutations are observed.

6.1. Andalusian Spanish
In Andalusian, aspiration and loss of /s/ occurs syllable-finally (including word-finally), which causes voicing of the following initial voiceless consonant (voicing assimilation). Example:

las casas /las ˈkasas/ → [lah ˈkaːsah] → [la ˈgaːsah] "the houses" (loss of /s/ and voicing of /k/ → /g/).

This resembles nasalization in Irish, where the loss of a final nasal caused voicing. However, in Andalusian the process is inconsistent and dialectally variable.

6.2. Canary Spanish
Similar phenomena, but with a greater tendency to preserve /s/ or replace it with [h]. Voicing is less pronounced.

6.3. Castilian Spanish
Standard Spanish: no mutations. However, historically there was lenition of intervocalic stops (e.g. Latin vita → Spanish vida [ˈbiða]), but it did not become grammaticalized.

7. Danish
Danish (North Germanic) demonstrates a phenomenon known as stød (glottal catch) — a special type of prosodic stress or glottalization, which can alternate in paradigms and resembles mutations in its grammatical function. Stød is opposed to its absence in some forms.

Examples:

hun [hun] "she" (without stød) vs. hund [hunˀ] "dog" (with stød).

mand [manˀ] "man" (sg.) vs. mænd [mɛn] "men" (pl., without stød).

Stød can occur in stressed syllables with a long vowel or sonorant. Although this is not a consonant mutation, typologically it is similar to Irish alternations: a phonetic change serves as a marker of grammatical categories (number, definiteness). The author draws a parallel: stød is a kind of "lenition at the prosodic level".

8. Baltic Finnic
8.1. Finnish
Finnish has consonant gradation, which affects the stops /p, t, k/ and their combinations in closed syllables (or conversely, in open ones). This is a morphophonological process similar to mutations, but inside a word, not initially.

Rules (basic):

Strong grade (in open syllable) → weak grade (in closed syllable):

/pː/ → /p/, /p/ → /v/ (or deletion)

/tː/ → /t/, /t/ → /d/ (or /l/, /r/, deletion)

/kː/ → /k/, /k/ → /∅/ (or /v/, /j/ depending on environment)

/pp/ → /p/, /tt/ → /t/, /kk/ → /k/ (in native words)

Examples:

kukka [ˈkukːɑ] "flower" (strong) vs. kukan [ˈkukɑn] "flower.GEN" (weak, /kː/ → /k/).

tapa [ˈtɑpɑ] "way" (strong) vs. tavan [ˈtɑvɑn] "way.GEN" (/p/ → /v/).

sata [ˈsɑtɑ] "hundred" (strong) vs. sadan [ˈsɑdɑn] "hundred.GEN" (/t/ → /d/).

Gradation is grammaticalized: it marks case, number, person of verb. This resembles Irish lenition, but operates inside a word (morpheme-internally) and depends on syllable structure, not on syntactic context.

8.2. Estonian
In Estonian, consonant gradation is more complex, including three lengths (short, long, overlong). Examples:

sada [ˈsɑd̥ɑ] "hundred" (short) vs. saja [ˈsɑjɑ] "hundred.GEN" (alternation /t/ ~ /j/).

kapp [kɑpː] "cupboard" (strong) vs. kapi [ˈkɑpi] "cupboard.GEN" (weak).

Estonian gradation is closer to Irish mutations in functional load, but is also internal.

9. Nivkh
Nivkh (Paleo-Asiatic language of Sakhalin Island and the mainland) has initial mutations strikingly similar to Celtic ones (convergent development). In Nivkh, stops and fricatives alternate depending on the final segment of the preceding word.

Examples (western dialect):

/p/ → /f/, /t/ → /r/, /k/ → /x/, /q/ → /χ/ after a vowel.

/p/ → /v/, /t/ → /d/, /k/ → /g/, /q/ → /ɢ/ after a nasal.

Example phrases:

pəχ "meat" → χə pəχ? — the author does not give precise phrases, but the scheme:

after a word ending in a vowel: tʰu fəχ "dog meat" (with lenition)

after a word ending in a nasal: hən dəχ "this meat" (with voicing)

In Nivkh, mutations are not only initial, but also in the middle of a word at morpheme junctures. This is one of the few non-Celtic languages with grammaticalized initial alternations. Phonetically, lenition p→f, t→r, k→x, q→χ; nasalization gives voiced stops. Difference from Irish: Nivkh has no palatality, but has uvular stops /q, ɢ/.

10. Fula
Fula (Niger-Congo family, Atlantic branch) has a complex system of initial consonant mutations that mark noun classes (like grammatical gender). Mutations include lenition, nasalization, preglottalization, and others.

Fula class system (examples with the "person" class):

Singular of different genders and plural alternate initial consonants:

gorko [ɡoɾko] "man"

gorkon [ɡoɾkon] "men"? — more precisely, usually:

class 1 (sg.): pullo "boy" /p/ → class 2 (pl.): pullo? No, in Fula:

lenition p → f, b → w, d → r, g → w, k → h, etc.

Specific examples (from the author's data):

debbo "girl" /d/ → pl. rebbe /r/.

gorko "man" /ɡ/ → pl. worko /w/.

pullo "boy" /p/ → pl. fullo /f/.

Fula also uses mutations to express definiteness, number, and case. This is one of the most complex mutation systems in the languages of the world, comparable to Celtic ones in grammatical load, but phonetically different (no palatality opposition, but labiovelars, etc.).

11. Southern Paiute
Southern Paiute (Uto-Aztecan family, USA) has a system of initial mutations that served as a subject of comparison with Irish in Kelly's dissertation (1978). In Paiute, alternations affect the initial consonants of nouns and verbs depending on grammatical context (e.g., the presence of a possessive pronoun).

Examples (from Kelly):

/p/ → /v/, /t/ → /r/, /k/ → /ɣ/, /kw/ → /w/, /m/ → /m/ (?)

paʼ "water" → a-vaʼ "his water".

tipo "rabbit" → a-ripo "his rabbit".

kwiŋi "pine" → a-wiŋi "his pine".

The Paiute system resembles Irish lenition, but operates more limitedly and is morphologically motivated. Nasalization is absent. Unlike Irish, Paiute has no palatal vs. non-palatal pairs.

12. Central Vanuatu languages
Languages of this group (e.g. Lenakel, Namakura) have initial mutations, which usually represent fortition (strengthening) after certain morphemes. Mutations affect sonorants and fricatives.

Example from Namakura:

ra "to sing" → after 2nd person sg. ko-ra → ko-tra (aspiration? Stop insertion).

la "to go" → a-la (3rd sg.) → a-tla (with /t/ insertion).

In Vanuatu, mutations are grammaticalized and serve to distinguish subject and object, tense, mood. These systems developed independently in Austronesian languages and represent a parallelism with Celtic and Nivkh.

13. Conclusion
The author summarizes the typological overview, highlighting the main parameters for comparing mutation systems.

13.1. Types of mutations
Lenition (weakening): stops → fricatives (Irish, Welsh, Scottish Gaelic, Breton, Nivkh, Fula, Southern Paiute, Tuscan Italian).

Nasalisation (nasal stop instead of oral or voiced stop): Irish, Welsh, Scottish Gaelic, Nivkh.

Fortition (strengthening): fricatives → stops (Breton, Vanuatu languages, limitedly in Finnish as the reverse grade).

Aspiration (voiceless fricative instead of a stop or vice versa): Welsh (aspirate mutation), Breton (spirant).

Prefixation of /h/ or /t/ (Irish, Scottish Gaelic).

Prosodic mutations (stød in Danish) — a rare type.

13.2. Weak and strong mutation
The author proposes to distinguish:

Weak mutation — a change that decreases the degree of closure or voicing (lenition, spirantization). Example: Irish lenition (p → f, t → h, k → x).

Strong mutation — a change that increases the degree of closure or adds nasality (nasalization, fortition). Example: Irish nasalization (p → b, b → m).

13.2.1. Exponence of weak mutation
Weak mutation is phonetically realized as:

Fricativization (p → f, k → x, t → h/θ)

Voicing (p → b, t → d) — if it is part of weakening (as in intervocalic position)

Loss (f → ∅)

13.2.2. Exponence of strong mutation
Strong mutation includes:

Nasalization (b → m, d → n, g → ŋ)

Aspiration (p → ph → f, but in Welsh this is a different type)

Fortition (f → p, θ → t, x → k) — occurs more rarely.

13.2.3. Two-stage and mixed mutation
In some languages (Welsh, Breton), two different mutations are possible in one context ("mixed mutation") — for example, after ei "her" in Welsh: cath → ei chath (aspiration), but after ei "his": ei gath (lenition). This shows that mutations can be lexically or grammatically distinct.

13.3. Diachronic developments
The author summarizes the paths of mutation origin:

Sandhi processes (intervocalic lenition, assimilation to a neighboring nasal) → grammaticalization.

Loss of final vowels or consonants (triggers) → mutation becomes the sole marker.

Analogical spread → mutation captures new contexts and lexemes.

Phonologization → mutated segments acquire the status of independent phonemes (like /ɣ/ and /x/ in Irish word-initially, which are not found in independent position).

In Celtic languages, mutations survived due to early grammaticalization and high functional status. In other languages (Italian gorgia, Andalusian Spanish), mutations remained at the phonetic level or are disappearing.

General conclusion to the fourth chapter
The fourth chapter shows that Irish is not unique in possessing initial mutations, but its system is one of the most complex and grammaticalized. The typological comparison reveals the following key points:

Lenition — the most common type of mutation, found in various languages of the world (Celtic, Nivkh, Fula, Southern Paiute, Tuscan Italian).

Nasalisation occurs almost exclusively in Celtic (and Nivkh) and is closely linked to historical nasal triggers.

Fortition is rare, characteristic of Breton and Vanuatu languages.

The opposition by palatality is a unique feature of Q-Celtic languages (Irish, Scottish Gaelic, Manx) and Slavic (Russian), but in Irish mutations, palatality is "invisible" (mutations apply to a pair of consonants regardless of its value).

The functional load of mutations in Irish is higher than in other Celtic languages, especially in distinguishing possessive adjectives "his/her/their" and marking verb tense.

Diachronically, mutations in Irish and Welsh developed in parallel, but in Irish they are more consistent and less subject to lexicalization.

The author concludes the chapter by asserting that studying mutations in a typological perspective helps to better understand both the uniqueness of Irish and the universal mechanisms of grammaticalization of phonetic processes.

4. The transcription of Irish
This section contains practical guidelines for using phonetic transcription in the book. Main principles:

Palatality is denoted by the diacritic [ʲ] after the consonant (e.g. [tʲ]).

Non-palatality (velarization) is denoted by [ˠ] (e.g. [t̪ˠ]), but in the book for simplicity, [t] without diacritics is often used if velarization does not contrast.

Neutral sonorants (only in western and northern dialects) – [lᵢ, nᵢ].

Vowel length — colon [ː] (e.g. [aː]).

Stress is usually not marked if it falls on the first syllable; in the southern dialect, stress may be indicated by a sign [`] before the stressed syllable (e.g. [kaˈlʲiːnʲ]).

Epenthetic vowel is transcribed as [ə] (e.g. bolg [ˈbˠʌlˠəg]).

Diphthongs – [aɪ, aʊ, iə, uə] (the book uses simplified notation /ai, au, iə, uə/).

Examples of transcription of one word in three dialects:

teacht "coming": North [tʲaxt̪], West [tʲaːxt̪], South [tʲaːxt̪] (difference in length of /a/).

am "time": North [am], West [aːm], South [aum].

leabhar "book": North [lʲoːɾˠ], West [lʲaʊɾˠ], South [lʲaʊɾˠ].

5.1. Linguistic features
Phonology:

The opposition of palatal and non-palatal consonants is preserved (as in Irish), but the three-way sonorant contrast is absent.

The distinction between /a/ and /ʌ/ is lost (in Irish they are distinct); Gaelic has a simpler vocalic system.

/iː/ after non-palatals is often retracted to [ɯː] (as in northern Irish).

/k/ and /kʲ/ are often pronounced with strong aspiration (in Irish, aspiration is weak or absent).

Initial mutations:
The system is almost identical to Irish, but there are some differences:

Lenition /p/ → /f/ (ph), /t/ → /h/ (th), /k/ → /x/ (ch), /b/ → /v/ (bh), /d/ → /ɣ/ (dh), /g/ → /ɣ/ (gh), /m/ → /v/ (mh), /s/ → /h/ (sh), /f/ → ∅ (fh).

Nasalisation: after mo "my", do "your", *a* "their", an "in": /p/ → /b/, /t/ → /d/, /k/ → /g/, /b/ → /m/, /d/ → /n/, /g/ → /ŋ/, /f/ → /v/ (in some dialects).

H-prefixation: after *a* "her" before a vowel: a h‑athair [ə ˈhahəɾʲ] "her father".

Examples from Gaelic:

cù /kʰuː/ "dog" (Ir. cú /kuː/ — without aspiration).

a chù /ə xuː/ "his dog" (lenition).

a gcù /ə kuː/ "their dog" (nasalisation — in Gaelic often without change, unlike Irish).

Differences from Irish:

Gaelic has lost neutral sonorants (all non-palatal sonorants are velarized, all palatal are palatal).

Gaelic has more contexts for lenition after prepositions.

In Gaelic, nasalization after mo "my" is preserved, while in Irish it has disappeared (in Irish mo triggers lenition).

Word order and verbal paradigms are close, but there are differences in tense and mood markers.

Example of lexical similarity:

Ir. teach /tʲax/ "house" — Gael. taigh /tʰɤj/ (with aspiration and diphthongization).

Ir. bó /bˠoː/ "cow" — Gael. bò /pɔː/ (with devoicing? Actually /b/ in Gaelic is partially devoiced? It is noted that initial voiced consonants in Gaelic are often half-voiced or voiceless unaspirated.)

Glossary
Although the glossary is not fully reproduced in the provided PDF (contains only the initial pages), in the book it includes definitions of the following key terms (given by the author at the beginning):

Base margin — the final part of the base subject to alteration.

Depalatalisation — change of a palatal consonant to a non-palatal as a grammatical marker.

Epenthesis — insertion of a vowel in heavy codas (sonorant+sonorant or sonorant+stop).

Geminate — historically long consonant (in Modern Irish, reflected on the preceding vowel).

Lenition — initial mutation in which a stop becomes a fricative (or /h/).

Metathesis — rearrangement of sounds (especially /r/ and /s/ with coronal stops).

Nasalisation — initial mutation leading to nasalization (b → m, d → n, g → ŋ) or voicing of voiceless stops.

Palatalisation — the process of changing the value of [palatal] from negative to positive in the syllable coda for grammatical purposes.

Polarity — the degree of palatality/velarization of a consonant (high, low, neutral).

Sonority — acoustic sonority, used to explain epenthesis and phonotactics.

Vowel gradation — change in quality of a short vowel depending on the palatality of the following consonant (e.g. /a/ → /e/ before a palatal).
