# Core Summary: Part I – Historical Background & Dialectal Phonetics

## 1. Historical & Orthographic Context for G2P
To accurately map modern Irish spelling to phonemes, an LLM must understand that Modern Irish orthography is deeply historical. The spelling often reflects older phonological stages rather than a direct 1-to-1 modern phonetic mapping.

*   **Lenition & Fricativization:** Stemming from Primitive/Old Irish, intervocalic consonants were weakened. Voiced stops fricativized (e.g., *gh, dh* $\rightarrow$ /ɣ/ and *bh, mh* $\rightarrow$ /v/). Over time, these fricatives were absorbed into preceding vowels, causing vowel lengthening or diphthongization.
*   **Vowel Coloring (Affection):** Old Irish developed a system where vowel height was influenced by the vowel in the following syllable. Because unstressed syllables were later lost (apocope/syncope), the phonetic weight of grammatical information shifted to the consonants, creating the **palatal (slender) vs. non-palatal (broad)** consonant distinction that dictates modern Irish phonology. 
*   **1948 Orthographic Reform:** Modern spelling can camouflage historical G2P triggers. For example, the modern word *saol* (/si:lɣ/ "life") was historically spelled *saoghal*. The silent `gh` (/ɣ/) was intervocalic, and its loss caused the surrounding vowels to merge into a long monophthong. The engine must treat sequences like `<ao>` as historical remnants of these mergers.

## 2. Dialectal Architecture
Modern Irish exists as a continuum broken into three primary dialect groups. A robust G2P engine must support parameterization for these three dialects, as their phonetic realizations of identical graphemes diverge significantly:
1.  **Northern Irish (Ulster/Donegal):** Characterized by initial stress, shortening of post-initial long vowels, fronting of back vowels, and affrication of palatal stops.
2.  **Western Irish (Connacht/Galway/Mayo):** Characterized by initial stress, retention of short+long vowel sequences, and distinct vowel lengthening before certain sonorants.
3.  **Southern Irish (Munster/Kerry/Cork/Waterford):** Characterized by variable word stress (attracted to non-initial long vowels) and heavy diphthongization before sonorants.

## 3. Explicit G2P Mapping Rules by Dialect (Section 2.3)

### A. Vowel Realizations
*   **The `<AO>` Grapheme:**
    *   **Rule:** Yields a long front monophthong. The consonant *preceding* it is always non-palatal (velarized/broad), giving the vowel a retracted quality.
    *   **Northern:** /i:/ (often retracted to [ɯː] or [λː] in Donegal).
    *   **Western:** /i:/ (sometimes lowered to /e:/ in formal contexts).
    *   **Southern:** /e:/.
    *   *Exceptions:* Some words trigger a diphthong /ai/ (e.g., *faoileán* $\rightarrow$ /failjɑ:nɣ/).
*   **Stressed Vowels before Syllable-Final Sonorants (L, N, R, M):**
    *   **Southern:** Triggers diphthongization (e.g., *am* $\rightarrow$ /au/).
    *   **Western:** Triggers vowel lengthening (e.g., *am* $\rightarrow$ /ɑ:/).
    *   **Northern:** Retains the short vowel (e.g., *am* $\rightarrow$ /a/).
*   **Unconditioned Long Vowels:**
    *   **Northern Shift:** `<á>` is heavily fronted to [æ:] or [ε:]. Mid-back vowels `<eo, ó>` are lowered (e.g., *bróg* $\rightarrow$ [brɔ:g]).
*   **Final Unstressed Short Vowels (e.g., `<a, e, i, o, u>` at the end of words):**
    *   **Southern & Western:** Reduced to schwa [ə] (e.g., *teanga*).
    *   **Northern:** Realized as a long [iː] in many regions.
*   **Long Vowels from Vocalized Fricatives:**
    *   **Rule:** `<agh, adh, abh, amh>`: The fricative moves from the coda to the nucleus, creating a long vowel or diphthong.
    *   *agh/adh* usually yields /ai/.
    *   *abh/amh* usually yields /au/ (especially in Southern Irish).

### B. Consonant Realizations
*   **Coronal Palatal Stops (tj, dj):**
    *   **Northern:** Realized as affricates [tʃ] and [dʒ].
    *   **Western:** Realized as palatal stops [tj, dj].
    *   **Southern:** Realized as alveolar stops [t, d].
*   **The Fricative `<ch>` /x/:**
    *   **Northern:** In pre-consonantal coda positions (like `cht`), it shifts to [r] (e.g., *ocht* $\rightarrow$ [art]). In word-final positions, it frequently disappears completely. 
    *   **Western/Southern:** Retained as a velar fricative [x].
*   **The Fricative `<bh/mh>` /v/ (before a non-palatal vowel):**
    *   **Northern & Western:** Shifts to the approximant [w] (e.g., *bhog* $\rightarrow$ [wʌg]).
    *   **Southern:** Retains labiodental friction [v] (e.g., *bhog* $\rightarrow$ [vʌg]).
*   **Sonorant Distinctions (N, L):**
    *   **Northern & Western:** Maintain a complex 3-way distinction: Velarized (non-palatal), Alveolar, and Palatal.
    *   **Southern:** Maintains only a 2-way distinction (Palatal vs. Non-palatal/velarized) with no distinct neutral alveolar form.
*   **Velar Stops in Post-Nasal Position (e.g., `ng`):**
    *   **Western (Cois Fharraige):** The stop is deleted, leaving only the nasal [ŋ].
    *   **Northern & Southern:** Retain the stop [ŋg].

### C. Prosody and Stress (Crucial for Vowel Reduction Mapping)
*   **Northern:** Strict initial stress. Post-initial long vowels are subject to extreme shortening (e.g., *scadán* $\rightarrow$ /ˡskadanɣ/).
*   **Western:** Strict initial stress. If a word has a short first vowel and long second vowel, the short initial vowel is raised to /ʊ/, but the short-long rhythm remains intact.
*   **Southern:** Variable stress. Long vowels or heavy syllables (like `-ach`) in non-initial syllables *attract* primary stress (e.g., *cailín* $\rightarrow$ [kaˡlji:nj]).

## 4. Implications for G2P System Architecture
To utilize this document effectively, your G2P engine should be programmed with the following sequential pipeline:
1.  **Syllabification & Stress Assignment:** Apply dialect-specific stress rules first, as this dictates whether a vowel is fully realized, reduced to schwa [ə], shortened, or if it triggers final-syllable diphthongization.
2.  **Palatality Tagging:** Evaluate the vowels flanking the consonants. Tag consonants as [+palatal] (slender, near `e/i`) or [-palatal] (broad, near `a/o/u`). 
3.  **Historical Digraph/Trigraph Resolution:** Parse `<bh/mh/gh/dh>` and `<ao/aoi>`. Apply the dialect-specific rules to determine if they become fricatives, glides [w], or are absorbed into the vowel to create length/diphthongs.
4.  **Sonorant Coda Checks:** Check if short vowels precede `ll, nn, m, rr, rd, rn`. If so, apply the dialectal divergence route (South $\rightarrow$ Diphthong, West $\rightarrow$ Long Vowel, North $\rightarrow$ Short Vowel).

# Core Summary: Part II – The Phonological Framework

## 1. The Master Rule: Palatal vs. Non-Palatal (Polarity)
The entire Irish consonant system is built on "polarity": every consonant (except `/h/`) exists as a pair—**Palatal** (slender, transcribed with superscript $^j$) and **Non-Palatal** (broad, strongly velarized, transcribed with superscript $^ɣ$).

### G2P Application for Polarity:
*   **Vowel-Driven Assignment:** The G2P engine must first scan the orthographic vowels flanking a consonant to assign its polarity. `e/i` = [+palatal], `a/o/u` = [-palatal].
*   **Secondary Palatals (Labials `P, B, M, F, V`):**
    *   Before *front* vowels, labials exhibit no on-glide (e.g., *mé* $\rightarrow$ `[mje:]`).
    *   Before *back* vowels, palatal labials exhibit a distinct `[j]` off-glide (e.g., *mion* $\rightarrow$ `[mjʌnɣ]`).
    *   Non-palatal labials before front vowels exhibit a velar glide `[w]` or `[ɯ]` (e.g., *buí* $\rightarrow$ `[bɯi:]`).
*   **Primary Palatals (Coronals & Velars):**
    *   Involve actual tongue-to-palate contact.
    *   **Northern dialect alert:** Palatal coronals `/tj, dj/` assibilate to affricates `[tʃ, dʒ]`.

## 2. Consonant Realizations & Contextual Rules

### A. Stops
*   **Final Devoicing:** In Western and Northern Irish, a palatal velar stop `/gj/` is devoiced to `[kj]` when it occurs at the end of an *unstressed* syllable (e.g., *Nollaig* $\rightarrow$ `[ˡnɣʌlɣɪkj]`).
*   **Voiced Velars in Southern Irish:** The historical final `/ɪj/` (often spelled `-igh`) closes into a voiced velar stop `[gj]` (e.g., *rachaidh* $\rightarrow$ `[ˡræçɪgj]`).

### B. Fricatives
*   **Sibilants (`S`):** Irish lacks voiced sibilants (`z, ʒ`). Non-palatal `s` is `[s]`; palatal `sj` is `[ʃ]`.
*   **The Glottal `/h/`:**
    *   When intervocalic and unstressed, `/h/` is frequently **deleted**, causing the preceding vowel to lengthen and absorb the syllable (e.g., *bóthar* $\rightarrow$ `[bo:r]`).
    *   When palatal `/sj/` or `/tj/` is lenited to `/h/` before a back/low vowel, it is realized as the palatal fricative `[ç]` (e.g., *thiocfadh* $\rightarrow$ `[çʌkəx]`).
*   **Velar Fricatives (`/x/, /ɣ/`):** 
    *   In the North, word-medial `/x/` (spelled `ch`) often weakens to `[h]`, and word-final `/x/` disappears completely. Before a `/t/`, it becomes `[r]` (e.g., *ocht* $\rightarrow$ `[art]`).

### C. Sonorants (L, N, R, M)
*   **The 3-Way Distinction (North/West):** `L` and `N` have three phonetic states: strongly palatal `[nj, lj]`, strongly velarized `[nɣ, lɣ]`, and neutral alveolar `[n, l]`. Southern Irish only uses a 2-way distinction.
*   **R-sounds:** Palatal `/rj/` in word-final position often assibilates to a voiceless fricative `[ʃ]` (West) or `[ʂ]` (e.g., *foclóir* $\rightarrow$ `[fʌklo:ʂ]`).
*   **Nasal Assimilation:** Dental nasals retract to velar `[ŋ]` before velar stops (`c, g`).

## 3. Vowel Gradation (Nucleus Assimilation)
In Irish, the quality of a **short vowel** is dictated strictly by the polarity of the consonant in the **coda** (the end) of its syllable, rather than the onset.

### G2P Application for Short Vowels:
*   **If Coda is [+palatal]:** The short vowel pulls front/high (`[ɪ]` or `[ε]`).
*   **If Coda is [-palatal]:** The short vowel pulls back/low (`[ʌ]` or `[a]`).
*   *Example:* *olc* (broad coda) $\rightarrow$ `[ʌlɣk]`. *oilc* (slender coda) $\rightarrow$ `[εljkj]`.
*   **Nasal Raising:** Short mid-vowels raise before nasals (`/o/` $\rightarrow$ `[u]`, `/e/` $\rightarrow$ `[i]`), e.g., *trom* $\rightarrow$ `[tru:m]`.

## 4. Epenthesis (Svarabhakti Vowel Insertion)
This is a highly predictable phonetic rule critical for G2P accuracy. An epenthetic vowel (usually a short schwa `[ə]` or `[ɪ]`) is automatically inserted to break up specific, phonotactically illegal consonant clusters.

### G2P Algorithm for Epenthesis:
Insert a vowel **IF** all of the following conditions are met:
1.  The cluster occurs in the coda of a **short, stressed syllable**.
2.  The cluster consists of **Sonorant** (`l, n, r`) + **Voiced Obstruent/Fricative** (`b, bh, v, m, g, gh`).
3.  The two consonants are **heterorganic** (made at different places of articulation).
    *   *Triggers Epenthesis:* *borb* (`r` + `b`) $\rightarrow$ `[bʌrəb]`, *seilbh* (`l` + `bh`) $\rightarrow$ `[ʃεljɪvj]`.
    *   *Blocks Epenthesis:* *ord* (`r` + `d` are homorganic coronals $\rightarrow$ no insertion); *olc* (`l` + `c` voiceless stop $\rightarrow$ no insertion); *téarma* (long vowel preceding $\rightarrow$ no insertion).
4.  **Vowel Selection:** Insert `[ɪ]` if the cluster is palatal; insert `[ə]` if the cluster is non-palatal.

## 5. Metathesis
Irish occasionally swaps the order of consonants to optimize syllable structure (usually moving a sonorant closer to the vowel).
*   **G2P Note:** This is mostly lexicalized today. The G2P should handle words like *trasna* (pronounced `[tarsnə]`) or *Béarla* (pronounced `[be:lɣrə]`) via dictionary exceptions rather than algorithmic rules, as the engine cannot safely predict which words trigger metathesis.

## 6. Syllable & Stress Algorithms (Expanded)
Chapter II confirms the stress paradigms outlined in Chapter I with precise phonotactic triggers:

### G2P Stress Assignment Logic:
*   **North & West:** Stress is fixed on the **1st syllable**. Unstressed vowels reduce to `[ə]` (or `[ɪ]` if flanked by palatals).
*   **South (Munster) Algorithm:**
    1.  Scan the word for long vowels or diphthongs.
    2.  If a long vowel exists in the 2nd or 3rd syllable, **shift primary stress** to that vowel (e.g., *cailín* $\rightarrow$ `[kaˡlji:nj]`).
    3.  If the word contains the suffix `-ach` or `-acht` `/ax(t)/` in the 2nd syllable, **shift stress** to that suffix (e.g., *portach* $\rightarrow$ `[pʌrˡtax]`).
        *   *Exception to #3:* Stress will *not* shift to `-ach` if the onset of that syllable is highly sonorous (like `r, l, h`), e.g., *ocrach* remains initial-stressed.
    4.  If both the 1st and 2nd syllables have long vowels, keep stress on the 1st syllable to avoid a metrical clash.

# Core Summary: Part III – The Morphonology of Irish

## 1. The G2P Architecture of Initial Mutations
Irish words possess "Base" (citation) forms that are dynamically altered at their left margins (word-initial) depending on the syntactic environment. The G2P engine must recognize these mutations as predictable phonological shifts, primarily **Lenition** (fricativization) and **Nasalization** (voicing/nasal assimilation).

### A. Lenition (Séimhiú)
Lenition fundamentally weakens a consonant by turning stops into fricatives. 
*   **G2P Rule Mapping:**
    *   `p` $\rightarrow$ `[f]`
    *   `b` and `m` $\rightarrow$ `[v]` (or `[w]` in North/West before broad vowels) -> *Note: `b` and `m` merge.*
    *   `c` (k) $\rightarrow$ `[x]` (or palatal `[ç]`)
    *   `g` and `d` $\rightarrow$ `[ɣ]` (or palatal `[j]`) -> *Note: `g` and `d` merge. The dental `d` shifts place of articulation to a velar `ɣ`.*
    *   `s` and `t` $\rightarrow$ `[h]` (or palatal `[ç]` before front vowels) -> *Note: `s` and `t` merge.*
    *   `f` $\rightarrow$ **Ø (Zero/Deleted)** -> *Note: The `f` becomes completely silent.*

### B. Nasalization / Eclipsis (Urú)
Nasalization occurs in two tiers depending on the voicing of the target consonant:
1.  **Voiceless Stops become Voiced:**
    *   `p` $\rightarrow$ `[b]`
    *   `t` $\rightarrow$ `[d]`
    *   `c` (k) $\rightarrow$ `[g]`
    *   `f` $\rightarrow$ `[v]` (Spelled `bhf`)
2.  **Voiced Stops become Homorganic Nasals:**
    *   `b` $\rightarrow$ `[m]`
    *   `d` $\rightarrow$ `[n]`
    *   `g` $\rightarrow$ `[ŋ]` (Spelled `ng`)

*   **Exceptions/Blocks:** Coronal sonorants (`l, n, r, m`) generally resist both lenition and nasalization in modern orthography/phonology. 

## 2. Consonant Prefixation (Prothesis)
Alongside mutations, Irish injects specific consonants to the front of vowel-initial (and sometimes `s`-initial) words based on grammatical gender, case, or tense. The G2P must treat these prefixes as part of the word's onset.

*   **Prefix `h` (e.g., *a haois* "her age"):**
    *   Structurally equivalent to "zero mutation" for vowel-initial words. It attaches directly to the vowel nucleus without a hyphen. 
*   **Prefix `t` (e.g., *an t-arán* "the bread", *an tslí* "the way"):**
    *   Attached to vowel-initial masculine nouns (nominative).
    *   Attached to `s`-initial feminine nouns (nominative), but **ONLY** if the `s` is followed by a vowel or a sonorant (`l, n, r`).
    *   *G2P Constraint:* The sequence `ts` in this context (`tslí`, `tseachtain`) is pronounced simply as `[t]`. The `t` fully overrides/replaces the `s`.
*   **Prefix `d'` (e.g., *d'fhág* "left", *d'imigh* "went"):**
    *   Used as a past-tense marker before vowels and before `f` (because `f` lenites to zero in the past tense, leaving a vowel-initial sound). The `d` becomes the phonetic onset.

## 3. Base Margin Alteration (Terminal Palatalization)
Irish morphology heavily relies on altering the right margin (coda) of a word to signal grammatical case (e.g., Genitive) or number (e.g., Plural).

*   **Palatalization as an Inflection:** A broad (velarized) final consonant becomes slender (palatalized). E.g., *cnoc* $\rightarrow$ *cnoic*.
*   **Depalatalization as an Inflection:** A slender final consonant becomes broad. E.g., *beoir* $\rightarrow$ *beorach*.
*   **G2P Impact (Nucleus Assimilation):** As established in Chapter II, this terminal polarity shift forces the preceding short vowel to alter its trajectory (Vowel Gradation). The G2P must map the orthographic vowel changes (e.g., `o` $\rightarrow$ `oi`) not as new independent vowels, but as the nucleus assimilating to the new palatal coda.

## 4. Advanced Palatalization Mechanics (The Russian Typological Comparison)
The text compares Irish to Russian to highlight how the G2P engine should handle secondary articulations (glides).

### A. Labials (P, B, M, F, V)
Because the tongue is not used to form labials, palatalization is achieved via secondary lip-tensing and glides.
*   **Palatal Labial + Back Vowel:** Requires a distinct `[j]` off-glide.
    *   *Example:* *peann* $\rightarrow$ `[pjɑ:nɣ]`.
*   **Non-Palatal (Broad) Labial + Front Vowel:** Requires a distinct velar `[w]` or `[ɯ]` glide to protect the broad consonant from the front vowel.
    *   *Example:* *buí* $\rightarrow$ `[bɯi:]`.

### B. The Grapheme `<AO>`
*   Phonologically identical in behavior to the Russian vowel `[ɨ]` (ы). 
*   It is a highly retracted vowel that **only** occurs after a non-palatal (broad) consonant. It acts as an absolute phonetic barrier, ensuring the preceding consonant remains velarized. 
*   *Example:* *saol* $\rightarrow$ `[si:lɣ]`.

### C. Primary Palatals (Velars & Coronals)
*   **Velars (`c, g`):** In Irish, palatal velars (`kj, gj`) do *not* assibilate into affricates (unlike Italian or Slavic). They remain true stops `[c, ɟ]`.
*   **Coronals (`t, d`):** As noted in previous sections, palatal coronals (`tj, dj`) *do* assibilate to `[tʃ, dʒ]`, but this is highly prevalent only in Northern (Ulster) dialects.

## 5. Mergers & Boundary Sandhi
*   **Compound Lenition:** When two words are combined, the second element is typically lenited (e.g., *dubh* + *fluich* $\rightarrow$ *dubhfhluich*).
*   **Homorganic Blocking:** If the final consonant of the first word and the initial consonant of the second word are made at the same place of articulation (homorganic), **lenition is blocked**. 
    *   *Example:* *ceann* + *dána* $\rightarrow$ *ceanndána* (Not *ceanndhána*).
*   **Double Fricative Ban:** Irish phonotactics strictly prohibit two fricatives in sequence. If a mutation or compound would create a fricative-fricative cluster, one is deleted, devoiced, or altered.

# Core Summary: Part V – Appendixes (System Inventories & Standards)

## 1. The Standardized Consonant Inventory & Pairwise Notation
For computational efficiency, the text advocates for a **pairwise notation system**. Because almost every consonant in Irish exists as a pair (Palatal vs. Non-Palatal), the G2P engine should process consonants as abstract cover symbols (uppercase) that are subsequently assigned palatality (`j` or `ɣ`) based on flanking vowels. 

**Rule of Mutation Invisibility:** Initial mutations apply equally to both members of a pair. The polarity (palatality/velarization) remains constant during a mutation. 

**The Pairwise Mapping Table:**
*   **Labials:**
    *   **P:** `/p/` (broad) vs. `/pj/` (slender)
    *   **B:** `/b/` vs. `/bj/`
    *   **M:** `/m/` vs. `/mj/`
    *   **F:** `/f/` vs. `/fj/`
    *   **V:** `/v/` vs. `/vj/`
*   **Coronals:**
    *   **T:** `/t/` vs. `/tj/` (assibilates to `tʃ` in North)
    *   **D:** `/d/` vs. `/dj/` (assibilates to `dʒ` in North)
    *   **S:** `/s/` vs. `/sj/` (`[ʃ]`)
*   **Velars:**
    *   **K:** `/k/` vs. `/kj/` (`[c]`)
    *   **G:** `/g/` vs. `/gj/` (`[ɟ]`)
    *   **X:** `/x/` (broad `ch`) vs. `/xj/` (slender `ch`, realized as `[ç]`)
    *   **ɣ:** `/ɣ/` (broad `gh/dh`) vs. `/ɣj/` (slender `gh/dh`, realized as `[j]`)
*   **Sonorants:**
    *   **N:** `/nɣ/` (velarized) vs. `/nj/` (palatal)
    *   **L:** `/lɣ/` (velarized) vs. `/lj/` (palatal)
    *   **R:** `/r/` (broad tap/trill) vs. `/rj/` (slender tap/fricative)
*   **Non-Paired:**
    *   **ŋ:** Velar nasal (occurs in mutations like `ng-`).
    *   **h:** Glottal fricative (no palatal counterpart).

## 2. The Standardized Vowel Inventory & Lexical Sets
The G2P engine must recognize that short vowels in Irish are heavily centralized and highly susceptible to the polarity of their surrounding consonants. Long vowels are stable. The text uses "Lexical Sets" (keyword standardizations) to map these.

### A. Short Vowels (Centralized)
*   **/ɪ/** (High-front): Keyword **FIOS**. Occurs before/after palatals. 
*   **/ε/** (Mid-front): Keyword **TE**.
*   **/a/** (Low-front/central): Keyword **SLACHT**. Backs to `[a]` near broad consonants; fronts to `[æ]` near slender consonants.
*   **/ʌ/** (Mid-back unrounded): Keyword **COR**. Represents orthographic `o` or `u` in broad environments. 
    *   *Contextual variants:* Raises to `[ʊ]` before velar stops (e.g., *rug*). Retains a rounded `[ɔ]` in Northern Irish before liquids (*olc*).
*   **/ə/** (Schwa): Keyword **BALLA**. The default unstressed vowel.

### B. Long Vowels (Stable)
*   **/i:/** Keyword **LÍON**
*   **/e:/** Keyword **MÉ**
*   **/ɑ:/** Keyword **TÁ** (Fronts to `[æ:]` or `[ε:]` in Northern Irish)
*   **/o:/** Keyword **BÓ**
*   **/u:/** Keyword **CÚ**

### C. Diphthongs
*   **/ai/** Keyword **AGHAIDH**
*   **/au/** Keyword **CABHAIR**
*   **/iə/** (Centring): Keyword **BIA**
*   **/uə/** (Centring): Keyword **CRUA**

## 3. Parsing Training Data: Traditional vs. IPA Transcription
If the G2P engine is trained on historical or traditional Celticist linguistic datasets (e.g., Wagner, de Bhaldraithe), it must map legacy notation to standard IPA.

*   **Traditional Palatality (The Prime):** Older texts use a prime mark ` ' ` to denote slender/palatal. The engine must map `C'` to `Cj`.
*   **Traditional Sonorants (Uppercase):** Older texts use uppercase letters to denote "tense" (strongly polarized) sonorants vs. lowercase for "lax" (neutral) sonorants.
    *   `N'` or `L'` = Strongly palatal `[nj]`, `[lj]`
    *   `N` or `L` = Strongly velarized (broad) `[nɣ]`, `[lɣ]`
    *   `n'` or `l'` = Weakly palatal `[nj]`, `[lj]`
    *   `n` or `l` = Neutral alveolar `[n]`, `[l]`

## 4. Cross-Linguistic Parameters (Scottish Gaelic)

*   *Examples:*
    *   *tiocfadh sé* (he would come): `/tjʌkəx/` + `/sje:/` $\rightarrow$ `[tjʌkətʃe:]`
    *   *bhíodh sé* (he used to be): `/vji:x/` + `/sje:/` $\rightarrow$ `[vji:tʃe:]`

### 2. Consonant Epenthesis (Stop Insertion)
We covered *vowel* epenthesis (inserting schwa between a sonorant and a stop, like *tarbh* $\rightarrow$ `[tarəv]`). However, the text also details **Consonant Epenthesis**. When two highly sonorous consonants (like `n` and `r`) are forced together across a syllable boundary, Irish phonotactics insert a phantom coronal stop (`t` or `d`) to bridge them.
*   **G2P Rule:** If `n` or `l` is immediately followed by `r`, insert a homorganic stop (`t` or `d`) between them.
*   *Examples:*
    *   *banrán* (grumbling) $\rightarrow$ `[bɑ:nɣ.trɑ:nɣ]` (Phantom `t` inserted).
    *   *anró* (hardship) $\rightarrow$ `[anɣ.tro:]`.
    *   *malrach* (youngster) $\rightarrow$ `[malɣ.drəx]` (Phantom `d` inserted).

### 3. The "Neutral" Sonorant Assignment (`[n]` and `[l]`)
We established that Northern/Western dialects have a 3-way distinction for `L` and `N` (Strongly Broad, Strongly Slender, and Neutral). The G2P engine needs the exact phonotactic trigger for when to output the "Neutral" alveolar `[n]` and `[l]` instead of the polarized versions (`[nɣ]` or `[nj]`).
*   **G2P Rule:** Neutral `[n]` and `[l]` **never** occur at the beginning of a word. They **only** occur intervocalically, immediately following a *short, stressed vowel* and preceding an *unstressed vowel*.
    *   *duine* (person) $\rightarrow$ `[dɪnjə]` (Neutral `n`).
    *   *baile* (town) $\rightarrow$ `[baljə]` (Neutral `l`).
    *   *(Contrast with word-initial):* *neart* $\rightarrow$ `[njart]` (Strongly palatal).

### 4. Vowel Lengthening Likelihood Hierarchy (Western Irish)
In Western Irish, short vowels lengthen before heavy sonorant codas (`nn`, `ll`, `m`). However, the engine should treat this not as a binary rule, but as a **probability hierarchy** based on the specific vowel, as there is natural variation.
*   **Tier 1: Low Vowels (Categorical/100%):** Low vowels *always* lengthen. 
    *   *crann* $\rightarrow$ `[krɑ:nɣ]`
*   **Tier 2: High Front Vowels (Highly Probable/90%):** High front vowels almost always lengthen.
    *   *im* $\rightarrow$ `[i:mj]`
*   **Tier 3: Mid-High Back Vowels (Variable/50%):** Short `o/u` before `nn/ll/m` fluctuates wildly, even within the same speaker.
    *   *tonn* (wave) $\rightarrow$ `[tʌnɣ]` OR `[tu:nɣ]`. Your engine can safely map this to either, but `[ʌ]` is safer for broader consistency.

### 5. Fossilized Permanent Lenition
Initial mutation is usually driven by syntax (e.g., following a possessive pronoun). However, a small set of highly frequent grammatical words underwent lenition historically and became "frozen" in that state. 
*   **G2P Rule:** Create a hardcoded exception dictionary for these words so the engine never outputs their unlenited orthographic root:
    *   *chun* (to/towards) $\rightarrow$ `[xʌnɣ]` (Never `[k...]`)
    *   *chomh* (as) $\rightarrow$ `[xo:]`
    *   *thú* (you - object pronoun) $\rightarrow$ `[hu:]` (Contrasts with subject *tú* `[tu:]`)
    *   *dhom/dhuit* (to me/to you - spoken variants of *dom/duit*) $\rightarrow$ `[ɣʌm]`, `[ɣɪtj]`.

### 6. Assibilation of `[h]` After `[r]`
In specific Northwestern dialects (like Erris/Mayo and parts of Donegal), the glottal fricative `[h]` undergoes a radical shift when it follows an `[r]`. The `r` causes the `h` to retract and harden into a "sh" sound `[ʃ]`.
*   **G2P Rule (Northwest toggle):** `r` + `th` (`[h]`) $\rightarrow$ `[rʃ]`.
    *   *gearrtha* (cut) $\rightarrow$ `[gja:rʃi:]` (Standard is `[gja:rhə]`).
    *   *threibh* (tribe - lenited) $\rightarrow$ `[ʃrjevj]`.

### 7. The "Akzentumsprung" (Accent Jump) Exceptions
There is a tiny class of words that look like they begin with a back vowel, but they trigger a palatal (slender) mutation on preceding words (like the article *an*). This is because historically, the stress "jumped" from a front vowel to a back vowel, but the palatal ghost remained.
*   **G2P Rule:** Hardcode these specific words to force preceding consonants to become slender:
    *   *iúl* (knowledge) $\rightarrow$ *an t-iúl* must have a slender `t`.
    *   *iúr* (yew) $\rightarrow$ *an t-iúr* must have a slender `t` (e.g., `[ənj tj u:r]`).

### 8. Modern Youth Speech & English Interference (Sociolinguistic Toggles)
If your engine aims to model contemporary/younger speakers, the text notes the infiltration of English phonotactics into modern Irish.
*   **The Retroflex `[ɻ]`:** Traditional Irish `r` is a tapped/velarized `[ɹ]` or `[rɣ]`. Modern youth speakers, influenced by Hiberno-English, map word-final broad `r` to the English retroflex `[ɻ]`.
    *   *Traditional:* *leor* $\rightarrow$ `[ljo:rɣ]`
    *   *Modern/Youth:* *leor* $\rightarrow$ `[ljo:ɻ]`
*   **Voiced Sibilants (`z` and `ʒ`):** Traditional Irish strictly devoices English loanwords containing `z` or `ʒ` (e.g., *zoo* $\rightarrow$ `[su:]`). Modern speakers, however, retain the English voiced sibilants in technical terms or code-switched names.
    *   *G2P Rule:* If the word is flagged as a modern loan (e.g., *ózón*), allow `[o:zo:nɣ]`.

### 9. Phonaesthetic "S" Prefixation
Irish sometimes adds an unetymological `s-` to the beginning of words to convey a negative, messy, or pejorative meaning. This `s` behaves as part of the root.
*   **G2P Concept:** The engine must treat the `s` in these specific words as a permanent onset, not an applied mutation. 
    *   *glugaí* (flabby person) $\rightarrow$ *sglugaí*
    *   *liobar* (tatter) $\rightarrow$ *sliobar*
    *   *cabiteir* $\rightarrow$ *scabhaitéir* (blackguard)
